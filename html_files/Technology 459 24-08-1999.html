Internet fits the bill for payments and news 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 24-08-1999

 THE question no longer is whether the Internet is useful, but what it is most useful for.
It cannot simply be viewed as a toy, although you can play and have fun on it.
It is not merely a place to perve on porn, but only fools would fail to acknowledge the sheer weight of naughtiness available.
And it is not just the domain of geeks who want to swap arcane source codes, or nerds who think it is a cool place to strut their latest networking knowledge. Of course it is more than a handy medium for both these pursuits.
But is any of the above useful in the everyday scheme of things?
As mundane as it may sound, the Net could soon become a useful place to do something as ordinary as pay bills.
Those who have used BPay telephone services know how dehumanising it is to be guided through the maze by a robotic voice.
You know the drill - If you know your name, hit 1; if you live in Perth, don't hang up.
All that to hand over your hard-earned to someone else.
Anyone who has performed BPay through an Internet banking service will appreciate the control and time given to the bill payer by comparison.
Unfortunately, not many companies offer BPay through online banks yet.
But, according to researchers www.consult, the tide is building for more reliance on e-billing, especially if we follow the US experience.
The researchers say 70 per cent of US households will pay bills electronically by 2009 compared with 4 per cent now. American utilities such as electricity companies, water companies and cable operators are expected to save $1.8 billion by using e-bills.
In Australia, the e-Bill company is about to launch an online payment system, while the Australian BPay Service uses its Web site to let consumers know the ins and outs of the procedure.
It is also developing an Internet billing and payment system, called Electronic Bill Presentment and Payment (EBPP), which it expects to complete early next year.
The ABC has also latched on to the Net as a useful place.
Radio Australia is using a special Web site to provide comprehensive coverage of the historic vote for independence in East Timor next week.
Its site is in both English and Indonesian and is based on the success of the special site set up for the Indonesian election in June.
The ABC says the site will provide news updates in English and Indonesian; text, audio and pictures from reporters on the ground; profiles of key political figures, features, history and an interactive map.
Closer to home, Hollywood Private Hospital in Nedlands is using Net technology to introduce Web-based bookings for operating theatre times.
As soon as a booking is made from a surgeon's rooms, it is entered into the hospital theatre booking system.
The system also allows the doctor to print out the theatre list, anaesthetists' list and in-patient list. It is designed to cut the number of phone calls to theatre from the doctors' rooms.
Hollywood's information systems manager, Katerina Andronis, said last week the system would be simpler for the hospital because there would be fewer steps to book an operation.
