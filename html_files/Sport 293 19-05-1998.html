Keeping the faith can test a fan's patience 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 19-05-1998

 SOMEONE wise once said football is a funny old game. Too true.
Why else would a band of apparently happy Blues supporters sing ?We Are the Navy Blues? just minutes after Carlton lost to West Coast on Sunday?
They warbled away while troops of perplexed Eagles fans shuffled past on their way out of Subiaco Oval.
Carlton had just lost its seventh straight match and West Coast had broken a four-game duck. Please explain, as Pauline Hanson might say.
Football support - even from the armchair - requires faith. The seemingly thrilled Carlton barrackers were obviously pleased to see their chosen team was not a complete joke as might have been thought after the previous week's 89-point thrashing by Port Adelaide.
The manner of the Blues comeback from a 44-point deficit to lose by just eight points would have renewed their faith.
What then could relight the fires for Eagles fans who almost saw a repeat of the choke against St Kilda, and whose side kicked abysmally?
Using childlike logic you could say that they really were eight goals better than Carlton and really looked a 20.12 team rather than a 12.20 one.
Unfortunately that explanation denies reality.
Or you could focus on coach Mick Malthouse's explanation. He said after the game: ?The side will ebb and flow from time to time, given the fact that we have had almost a 50 per cent turnover in personnel in the last three years.?
The Eagles have had to find about 16 new faces since 1995, according to a list of everyone who has represented the team on display at the club's official Internet site.
Names from 1995 no longer with them include Peter Sumich, Peter Wilson, David Hart, Michael Brennan, Karl Langdon, David Hynes, Don Pyke and Brayden Lyle.
You can throw in Brett Spinks, Jason Heatley and Shane Bond too.
However, 13 members of the team that just beat Carlton on Sunday were around in 1995. And 18 of the 22 were listed as having played in 1996.
Significantly, Andrew Donnelly, Ben Cousins and Fraser Gehrig were all regulars that year which was marred by long absences by Glen Jakovich and John Worsfold because of knee reconstructions. A key missing element from 1996 is Chris Mainwaring, due to resume soon. Mitchell White, another 96 trump card, is possibly not far off a proper return.
Now that 1996 bunch lost four games on the trot, then won 11 straight to finish the year with a 15-7 record, 125 per cent and in fourth place.
Would it be too much of a leap of faith for Eagles supporters to expect a similar turnaround this year? Statistically speaking, it appears reasonable.
But has there been a more even competition? There are no easybeat Fitzroys or Melbournes. Richmond is roaring. The Bulldogs are giving their backers faith that they might see the club's second flag.
Teams in a similar boat to West Coast include Adelaide, Geelong, Essendon and Brisbane.
Something has got to break soon.
Let's hope it is not the faith.
