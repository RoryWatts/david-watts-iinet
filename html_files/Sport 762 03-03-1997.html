All wired up in first of super bore 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 03-03-1997

 WAR can be such a bore. Super League came to TV on Saturday night amid marketing hype not seen since the days of World Series Cricket.
At the heart of the saturation campaign was an ad which had several boofy blokes in shorts tearing madly through constantly changing domestic scenes.
The loud musical accompaniment was the Frankie Goes to Hollywood hit of several years ago, When Two Tribes Go to War.
All this and close to unquestioning coverage from 70 per cent of Australia's newspapers - which just happen to be owned by Rupert Murdoch, the tycoon who decided rugby league was just the thing to boost ratings on his TV networks round the world.
And the result: a scrappy, uninteresting match between Brisbane and Auckland that left the 42,000 crowd at Brisbane's ANZ stadium muttering with boredom and disinterest.
A media mogul like Mr Murdoch might control a competition but he can never control the game.
That is the preserve of the players and the conditions.
Auckland looked disinterested and uncoordinated like a team of individuals who'd just been introduced and told they had to put on a show against Super League's hottest prophets.
The Brisbane Broncos did just enough to ensure their 14-2 was a comfortable win.
There were complaints that the ball was slippery and conditions humid, though not a million miles away the ACT beat Queensland in the Super 12s rugby union match at Ballymore.
Queensland skipper Tim Horan told Channel 7 yesterday conditions were ideal and the ball was never slippery.
Super League at ANZ appeared to have everything.
There were the hottest rock acts, Yothu Yindhi and Belinda Carlisle, the hottest fireworks, and the hottest rivalry - an Australian team and a NZ team.
So what was the lasting impression from this hot new would-be star of television sport?
The never-ending nagging of referee Bill Harrigan.
Some genius thought it would be a good idea to have the referee wired for sound so TV viewers could hear his every breath.
The whole purpose of refereeing is to be as inconspicuous as possible.
The crowd and TV viewers want to watch sport being played, not hear the priceless words of officialdom.
The referee's microphone dominated all other sound.
The biggish crowd (stadium capacity is 50,000; so it was the equivalent of about 30,000 at Subiaco) was hard to hear.
Commentators Warren Boland and Wally Lewis had most of their words overshadowed by the continual ?get off him; back, back; that's four?, and they did not sound impressed.
Even after the lacklustre game, the fireworks were dragged out again.
The noise seemed to startle Buck the horse which parades around on behalf of the Broncos.
He high-stepped like a dressage entrant.
His rider dropped his Broncos flag, then lost his Akubra, then appeared to lose his cool.
Far better TV value was Channel 7's impressive line-up of Fremantle proving far too good for a nervous-looking Port Power in the Ansett Cup; the Super 12s rugby union thriller between Waikato and Wellington; the Three Tenors from LA; and the two Matts (Elliott and Hayden) from Johannesburg in the absorbing if horribly delayed first Test.
