Barry pens tale on the Web 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 24-09-1996

 THE best book about computers and the Internet is not on paper yet, nor is it particularly accurate or scientific.
But American humorist Dave Barry certainly hits some home truths in his wicked look at the history of computing called A Brief History of Computing: From Cave Walls to Windows95 (Not That This is Necessarily Progress).
The 14 chapters are being presented one by one on the World Wide Web every Monday, Wednesday and Friday. By yesterday he had dispensed with eight.
Barry, whose syndicated Miami Herald column used to appear in The West Australian, does not let facts get in the way of a good story. But this irreverent approach coupled with his zany sense of humour - he is the world expert on seeing how far a Roller Barbie doll will travel when set alight - should not stop computing professionals and purists.
In his first chapter, Man, the Computing Animal, Barry claims: ?The first human beings didn't need computers, because they had no numbers.
?This was a big problem for parents, because they had no way to control their children ("You kids stop that! I mean it! I'm going to count to ... um ... to ...')
Other chapters include The Alternative World of Apple, a computer Barry says is fit only ?for drug addicts and Barry Manilow? because it actually responds to the user rather than setting them a titanic struggle like Windows.
The book will vanish from cyberspace on October 9.
