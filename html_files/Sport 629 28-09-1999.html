And the winner is ... NRL 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 28-09-1999

 SO who won the battle of the grand finals - the AFL or the NRL?
Putting aside all bias towards the free-flowing athleticism of football against the up-and-down-the-field predictability of rugby league, the vote would have to go to the NRL at Stadium Australia on Sunday.
The match was watched by a world record rugby league crowd of more than 107,000. The AFL attracted just under 95,000. It has been more than 50 years since league outdrew football on its big day.
The Storm's 20-18 win over St George-Illawarra was one of high drama. It got within striking distance courtesy of a pass that looked forward.
It tied the score at 18-all three minutes from the end because of that rare beast, the penalty try. There has not been a grand final penalty try for 22 years.
Melbourne's triumph can be described as a fairytale, although that word is used advisedly not least because it is applied to the actions of some big, boofy blokes such as Glenn Lazarus.
The team has existed for only two seasons and represents an Aussie rules-mad city that before Sunday was only vaguely aware of its existence.
Though both channels 7 and 9 appear to have spent the gross national product of a Third World country in bringing the grand finals into our homes, Nine must be judged the winner because its coverage was commercial-free.
Where Nine used swags of super slow-motion replays to analyse how tries were scored - or whether they were scored - Seven reserved its heaviest use for Justin Murphy's agonising knee injury.
And Seven started its grand final coverage on Friday night with a mostly Victorian look at the AFL competition during the 90s.
Called the Decade That Delivered, it glossed over the fact that West Coast dominated football for three or four years by showing highlights only of losses.
Indeed it was a total surprise to see the Eagles in three grand finals after the footage appeared to make them such easybeats.
Some less sober critics such as Victorian Premier Jeff Kennett have claimed that the Storm's NRL win is further proof that Victoria is truly the home of all football.
But the Storm is a franchise team. It has no Victorian players. Five of the squad, including Sunday stars Rodney Howe and Matt Geyer, played for the now-dead Perth Reds.
The Storm was born during the Super League fiasco and can thank the News Corporation empire of Rupert Murdoch - which owns 50 per cent of the NRL - for its existence.
It is about as Victorian as Alfie Langer and rugby league is about as traditional to Melbourne as bobsledding.
Sydney rugby league critic Malcolm Knox put it this way: ?It shows you can go to a place where bugger-all people follow the game ... and throw bagfuls of money at a team, and it doesn't matter that they're not a real club, just part of a company ... and yet they can still win a comp.?
I think he barracks for St George.
