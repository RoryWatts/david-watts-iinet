Meatware revels in the power 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 17-10-1995

 MEATWARE is the human side of technology.
The US magazine Wired, in its Jargon column in August, defined meatware as the computer component needed to interface with hardware and software.
As technocrats are wont to do, the column bemoaned meatware as the component most likely to get things wrong.
Certainly the human element is responsible for most of the fun and nearly all the unpredictability of technology.
The term ?paperless office? gained a lot of favour in the late 1970s when many businesses were dipping their toes in the technological ocean.
Today, of course, you can barely see the mainframes for the printers. Documents have gone feral. Colour and copies are all the rage.
Here are a few more examples of meatware on the rampage:
The managing director of a big multinational corporation, based in Melbourne, needed to get to a meeting in a hurry - time's money and all that.
As he was about to sprint from his office, his computer buzzed to say there was urgent intraoffice e-mail.
He started reading what looked a very important document. The first six screens listed addressees from head office executives to reasonably important minions from Karratha to Cairns. The last screen contained the message: ?The company choir will be practising Christmas carols in the canteen on Tuesday. If you're in the area please drop in.?
A Perth legal beagle was about to rush to court for an important hearing when his e-mail alert went off. He looked at the screen to see the apocalyptic message: ?The tea club is running short of money. All employees will have to bring their own milk in future.?
The legal one reckons that technology has put the real power into the hands of a group of spotty 18-25 year-olds.
A Perth man who prides himself on his taste in movies was about to award Six Degrees of Separation 8 out of 10 on the Internet Movie Database when his six-year-old son demanded he give Power Rangers, the Movie 10 out of 10 ... or he wouldn't eat his tea.
At a quality London newspaper, the computer system was taking ages to do simple tasks.
One of the Oxbridgeeducated reporters sent the Cockney system controller a message, saying: ?I say, it's a bit like Waiting for Godot up here old chap.?
The system controller messaged back: ?Who's this bleedin' Godot then? Has he got a logon??
