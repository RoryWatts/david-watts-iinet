Players must perform a role - a TV role 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 19-01-1999

 TELEVISION has turned sport into a vast arena of great expectations.
Not that the field of human combat was not already under such strain.
My grandfather used to tell me the streets of Brisbane would empty when the wireless breathlessly announced Bradman had gone in to bat. The expectation was that the master would make a century and entertain the populace.
The outcome was that he nearly always did. But wool was on its way to being worth a pound a pound and you could leave your back door unlocked, too.
Now, TV, with a combination of slow motion, tilted camera angles and music, is trying to turn sport into something bigger than and different to the huge distraction it always has been.
Look no further than Channel 7's coverage of the first day of the Australian Open yesterday.
The relentless home crowd pressure is on Pat Rafter or Mark Philippoussis to be there at the end. To a lesser extent, a lot is also expected of Lleyton Hewitt and Jelena Dokic.
Seven - in pursuit of sport as another blockbuster program - intensified the pressure with a segment showing Philippoussis playing at super slow motion (to accentuate his power no doubt) and to the accompaniment of Frank Sinatra's Let Me Try Again.
If one viewing was not stomach-churning enough, Seven put it on again at the end of its daytime coverage to highlight the main game of the night.
This daytime cover featured our great hope, Rafter, winning handsomely against German Oliver Gross after the usual early hiccups.
It also featured a tiny segment of Rafter smashing away to the accompaniment of grand classical music.
Does the difference in soundtracks make Rafter the classic hero and Philippoussis the sentimental favourite?
Will Hewitt be given a few verses of Blond Headed Stompy-Wompy Real Gone Surfer Boy?
And young Dokic treated to some lines of William Blake - tiger, tiger burning bright?
Tennis on TV seems less and less to be about the game. More and more a parade of healthy, young personalities who the public see as celebrities.
One Seven interview with spectators yesterday featured a bloke in a cocktail dress, camping it up that he had come only to see former champion Mary Pierce, now better known for her dramatic on-court antics than her hard-hitting game.
Why else would Russian super-brat Anna Kournikova be such a crowd puller? The 17-year-old barely wins a match, but she looks good in a tight tennis outfit.
