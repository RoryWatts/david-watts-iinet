Reeltime turns into real time on RealVideo 

Author: NATHAN COCHRANE; DAVID WATTS

Date: 18-02-1997

 INTERNET surfers will be able to view Fox broadcasts free before most people have access to the Foxtel cable network thanks to new technology unveiled last week.
The RealVideo system was developed by US Internet software company Progressive Networks for real-time streaming of sound and video on the World Wide Web. Previous systems required that whole video clips be downloaded to a user's computer before they could be played, a time-consuming process. RealPlayer clips are highly compressed and can be played and downloaded simultaneously.
Fox News, the US parent company to Australia's Foxtel cable-TV network, which started in Perth earlier this month, is one of the first partners to provide real-time audio and video content for the service.
The exclusive world premiere of three Spike Lee films took place last week using the system.
?Lee's efforts were designed to demonstrate the wide variety of compelling content that can be produced for Web users,? Progressive said.
Other US companies supporting the RealVideo format include C-Span, ABC, Warner Bros, Virgin, and Touchstone Pictures.
Foxtel continues to push past WA homes, but some users have complained of long delays to get connected. Foxtel claims to be turning on about 1000 homes a week. At launch only 1000 homes were connected.
A Foxtel spokesman said last week that delays of up to three months are likely in some areas even if there was already cable in the street.
In the meantime, Realvideo is the next best thing, and it is free.
RealVideo is the younger, but more visually tempting, brother of RealAudio, one of the real star applications on the Net that allows live streaming, audio broadcasts - speeches, sports commentary, interviews and music.
As its name says, RealVideo is designed to do the same with video.
It works too, but is a little like the early snow-plagued days of television.
RealVideo can be set up for the average 28.8kbps modem user on a dialup network, but replays are subject to fuzziness, delays, blackouts and total non-appearance. Congestion on servers' lines is mainly responsible, and RealVideo alerts users to that fact in a window under its playback screen.
It really is crystal-radio stuff - fascinating that it can be done, but actually lacking in the qualities expected of television.
The picture itself is like stop-gap animation, totally out of synch with the good quality audio.
However, RealVideo is there because in the not-too-distant future the speed at which we hook up to the Net will be much higher - that is the bandwidth will be bigger.
?RealVideo is optimised for today's real world bandwidth capacities. RealVideo delivers newscast-quality video over 28.8 kbps modems, full-motion-quality video using V.56 (56 kbps) and ISDN (56/64 kbps) modems, and near-TV broadcast quality video at LAN rates or broadband speeds (100 kbps and above),? Progressive says.
The maker might boast of newscast-quality at 28.8kbps, but it is not wonderful. However, many video presentations can be buffered to deliver better quality after a few minutes' delay.
RealVideo is certainly far faster than many video players which require big files to be downloaded for a few seconds play.
And if the experience of RealAudio is anything to go by, the quality should get better and better.
RealVideo is played on the RealPlayer which overtakes the RealAudio player. Users wishing to update RealAudio will now have to download RealPlayer which will give both audio and video.
