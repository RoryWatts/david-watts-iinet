There's a catch in the cricket lover's delivery 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 20-11-1998

 SOME time today, if the Brisbane weather is kind, someone will break my reverie in front of the television to ask: ?Who's winning the cricket?? I will say, ?we are? and pray for no more interruptions, however friendly or well-intentioned.
When I was younger and more patient, I used to take my eyes off the screen and fix them on the questioner to explain at length . . . ?At this stage of the game, it's impossible to tell who's winning. England has just started bowling, the Australian openers look vulnerable, the wicket is two-paced and I'm sure the humidity is making benign-looking deliveries unplayable.
?In fact, it is often hard to say, even in the last hour, who's winning. The game is complicated. There's the weather, the conditions, the umpires.
?Moreover, many games are never actually won. They are drawn, like a stalemate in chess . . . ? Then the questioner would look dazed and say: ?Yeah, but who's winning??
I would look back at the screen to discover four wickets had fallen, the frightening fast bowler had been no-balled for throwing and a seagull had taken off with the bails.
It is hard work loving cricket. Many hours are spent watching what looks like the identical scene being repeated. The medium-pacer trundles (they never run or walk) in, rolls his arm over. The ball is on a good length just outside off-stump and the batsman lets it through untouched to the keeper.
And again, and again . . . for five days if you're lucky.
All this is endured in the hope of a few marvellous moments such as a Shane Warne delivery spinning a metre to clean bowl Mike Gatting or a Justin Langer back-foot drive hit so thunderously, it rockets into the boundary before the fieldsmen can move.
It is a game of raptures and time
wasting and marvellous trivia.
The other day I discovered on the Internet the answers to questions I've asked secretly for years (one does not admit lack of knowledge in cricket matters). Someone - the misguided fool - wanted to know how the fielding positions got such quaint names like point, gully, slips, and so on.
And someone, from India I think, was so tragically in love with the game that he claimed he knew, and served up these:
POINT: In the early days of cricket, point denoted a very close position. Its full name - the point of the bat - indicates that the fielder stood very near to the end of the striker's bat.
THIRD MAN: First appeared in the mid-19th century to describe a position to supplement the more established close off-side positions of point and short slip. The new man was thus the third man up.
SLIP: Originally the long stop, who ?is required to cover many slips from the bat?.
GULLY: Suggests the narrow channel between point and the slips.
COVER: Literally a fieldsman who covers for those closer in.
Now this man may be talking through his washing hat but those answers satisfy me . . . until something more intriguing and equally unsourced comes along.
After all, who wants the facts to stand in the way of a romantic game?
AFTER last week's column - an e-mail to Prince Charles - a reader told me to ?stick to your normal inanities rather than the pretentious twaddle published today?.
So I will. The prince received some fabulous presents for his 50th birthday last Saturday. He was promoted to two-star rank in all three military service branches: Major-General in the army, Rear-Admiral in the navy and Air Vice-Marshal in the air force.
The Times felt moved to Gilbert and Sullivan style verse:
Prince Charles he is the model of a modern Major-General. To his birthday he's invited the glitterati one and all.
And to give him brighter scrambled egg to wear upon his epaulette, His promotions are announced today in the London Gazette. For he's also made Rear-Admiral and Air Vice-Marshal too, Rising faster through the Services than lesser men can ever do.
Inane enough for you?
