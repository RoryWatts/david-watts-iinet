Parents get choice of Net porn blocks 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 12-03-1996

 PARENTS now have a wide variety of ways to block their children's access to what they consider unsavoury Internet sites.
The growing push against Net censorship in the US and Australia has, paradoxically, unleashed an increasing number of ?filth blockers?.
This software is available for trial downloads all over the Net, and for as little as $30 parents have a choice.
With names such as Net Nanny, Surf Watch, Cyber Patrol and CYBERsitter, there can be no doubting the self-regulatory flavour and fervour of the software, much of which has come into vogue during the long debate about US regulation of the Net.
A similar argument is brewing in Australia as the Australian Broadcasting Authority considers submissions about regulation of online services.
Outcries about the availability of pornography have prompted the ABA inquiry.
Assertions that Net users are responsible enough to regulate content without intervention from government are equally responsible for the outbreak of blocking software.
Cyber Patrol software, the most effective I have tested so far, promises it can block access to more than 7500 areas with sexually explicit material.
It has a daunting user interface at first, but this is because it is so flexible.
Parents or teachers can set the hours that the software can control access to the computer, build in their own no-go sites and override Cyber Patrol's own no-go areas if it is thought they are so stringent as to actually block good material.
The software's strength can be seen by the fact that US telecom giant AT&T chose it to be a built-in package for its 4.3 million WorldNet users.
Online giants CompuServe and Prodigy have also built Cyber Patrol into their Net packages.
Cyber Patrol's effectiveness can be seen in this little trial:
I typed ?nude celebrities? into a search engine and was given a list of sites that promised either a nude, a celebrity or a nude celebrity. Cyber Patrol would not let me access any of those sites.
The mere mention of sex, nudity and the like is enough for Cyber Patrol to deny access.
The blockers apply to FTP sites, IRC lines and especially to Usenet newsgroups, perhaps the sites containing most explicit material.
