Banks, Laws maintain silence on Web sites 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 27-07-1999

 ALL the players in the John Laws controversy use the Web to put their opinions.
But neither Laws nor the Australian Bankers Association has a word to say about the issue in their slices of cyberspace.
Laws might be facing several inquiries about taking money to stop criticising banks, but appears to have chosen not to defend himself at his Web site called John Laws' Fortress.
A page devoted to weekly highlights of his 2UE radio program was last updated on April 16. A spokesman for Toyota, which is responsible for maintenance of the site, told the Australian PC magazine last week the site was updated only when a new story from Laws came through.
The site is open to feedback and e-mails from Laws' listeners are posted on a page called Have Your Say.
Again, not a word about the banks, despite the page's blurb which says: ?We will be updating and featuring your reviews and comments on a regular basis.?
The section has listeners' comments on topics as diverse as country music, cricket, mateship and the Kosovo refugees. At least a dozen of the 50 published letters deal with Toyota ownership as well as the use of the word ?bugger? in a Toyota television commercial.
Toyota is both a sponsor of Laws' radio program and of the Fortress site.
The Australian Bankers Association runs a site which deals mainly with PR releases that put the banks' views.
Like Laws' site, the bankers appear not to have judged the controversy newsworthy.
At the weekend, the most recent news releases - those tagged with a ?new? image - appeared to be published in May.
Topics included: Bank customers misled, banking industry applauds promotional funding, industry welcomes tax package and customers lose if GST defeated.
A site with a lot to say about the Laws case is the one run by Perth talkback radio host Howard Sattler.
Unlike Laws and his station 2UE - which is still building a Web site - Sattler and his station 6PR are active Web users.
For more than 18 months Sattler has used his site virtually as an adjunct to his radio show. His front page features the latest topics discussed on-air and the site runs regular opinion polls which allow users to vote on current issues.
The Laws controversy is treated in depth under a headline titled ?Golden tonsils tarnished?.
Sattler says Laws has given his critics plenty of ammunition with which to work ?with all manner of vitriol and little contrary evidence to shake them off his tail?.
He claims the main issue in the controversy is whether a broadcaster has been selling his on-air editorial comment.
Sattler has also had allegations made against him, and again has used his site to put his side of the case.
On a page called ?The saga continues? he accuses journalists and their newspapers of ignorance for reports that have linked him to the Laws scandal.
And in an appeal to his Web audience, he says: ?Only you will determine my future ... and if you believe I have let you down it is you who will remove me by simply switching off the radio.?
The Australian Broadcasting Authority, which has announced inquiries into the Laws-banks deal, has published details in full at its What's New section.
The regularly updated page deals with a wide range of communication issues. At the weekend, two news releases about the Laws inquiries were sandwiched between others dealing with local content on TV, a community radio licence for Tamworth and the release of digital TV channels.
