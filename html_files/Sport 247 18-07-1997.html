In search of the real bossy boots 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 18-07-1997

 IT HAS been a quiet week and a cold one; the perfect time to try to prove the existence of God.
Three little examples have helped my quest in the past seven days. They are all quite different and at least one perhaps a little ungodly. Let's start with it.
Last Sunday God smiled on Subiaco Oval. It may not have been apparent to the casual observer because it rained buckets and in the best wintry fashion was cold . . . cold as hell as they say.
But God smiled on Mick Malthouse's patched-up, bedraggled team of battling Eagles. And they did win.
The second piece of evidence emerged down south on Tuesday. It was sunset at Gracetown, a near perfect winter's day - light winds, light cloud, light beer. The surf was up, rolling to shore in continuous lines that stretched from one side of the bay to the other.
Exhausted grommets sat on their cars staring at the majesty. They had been out there among the two metre waves all day, frolicking in paradise . . . and wet suits, because it was colder than a penguin's tummy.
Now they watched the sun drop from behind a small white cloud on the horizon. Plop, it seemed to go as it disappeared quickly into the rolling ocean. You could almost hear it simmer as it submerged.
At the same time I had one ear to the car radio. The ABC handed me the next example of God on a collection plate, as it were.
Amid rumblings about Pauline Hanson's divisiveness was a little news item about an Anglican vicar in England.
Rev Alex Coker, 48, had lost his fight to take a claim of unfair dismissal to an industrial tribunal. Dr Coker, once the curate at St Philip's Church in Cheam, south-west London, was sacked in May 1994 without being given any reasons.
HOWEVER, the Court of Appeal has ruled that Dr Coker - indeed all clergy - are not protected by employment laws because they work for God rather than any earthly power.
?A minister of religion serves God and his congregation but does not serve an employer,? said Lord Justice Staughton.?There is not a contract that he will serve a terrestrial employer in the performance of his duties.?
So there we have it, the ultimate boss. One wonders what precedents will be set. Can workers now tell their human bosses to ?shove it? when refused a pay rise, and go to a higher power? Could Sundays be off limits again? Could Graham Kierath's various waves of industrial legislation come crashing down because he has not consulted an ultimate power?
All this talk of the search for God reminds me of two jokes. I presume God has a sense of humour. Why else would He have created camels, or ducks, or Pauline Hanson for that matter?
The first is: Did you hear about the dyslexic, agnostic insomniac?
He lies awake at night wondering if there is a dog.
The second concerns a man who dies suddenly. The bloke is a little taken aback. He did not expect to shuffle off the mortal coil so early, particularly as he has so little to show for his life.
But he's gone and that's that. He finds himself at the Pearly Gates in the presence of St Peter.
St Peter looks down on him and says: ?My son you have not much to show for yourself so I need you to perform one small, simple test to gain entrance to Heaven. You must take this footy and kick a Peter Matera-style spiral torpedo straight through the middle of the Pearly Gates 50m away.?
The bloke is somewhat worried. He was never a very good kick. But he has a go because the alternative is to play for the Demons . . . forever.
He lines up and praise be it's a beauty, bang on line. But just as the ball is about to go through the gates, a tall bearded figure leaps spectacularly and marks it.
?Is that God?? the bloke asks in astonishment.?Don't talk so loud, he thinks he's Gary Ablett,? replies St Peter.
