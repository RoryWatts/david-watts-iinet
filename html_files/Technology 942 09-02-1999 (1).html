Net police issue splits users: poll 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 09-02-1999

 THE $168 million penalty against anti-abortionists who published a ?hit list? of doctors on the Web has sparked renewed debate about whether the Internet can be policed.
A snap poll of Net users by the BBC last Thursday found 51 per cent thought it could be.
The poll's proposition was that it was commonly thought the Net was associated with freedom of expression.
But after a federal jury in Portland, Oregon, found that wanted-style posters of abortion doctors on the Nuremberg Files site amounted to death threats, defendant Catherine Ramney said: ?This is a moral and constitutional outrage. There was no threat and they knew it.?
This outburst added to controversies and law suits against Ku Klux Klan and other white supremacist sites, the BBC said at a special site set up for comment.
?There is a strong culture that believes the Net as a medium should represent every kind of view and information,? the site said.
But it was also recognised that certain material should be filtered to protect children.
Dilemmas in the online world were growing.
In the same week that the anti-abortionists were fined, a US judge ruled that the Child Online Protection Act - designed to penalise sites that made pornography accessible to minors - was unconstitutional on the ground of the right to freedom of speech.
Some reactions from Web surfers who voted in the poll:
?How do you police a global network whilst each country has its own set of laws? What is legal here isn't somewhere else, and vice versa ... I police myself quite adequately thanks.?
?It cannot and should not be policed. If you don't like what you see, don't look.?
?An international standards body should draw up a set of minimum standards based on current film censorship standards and those sites infringing these should have their IP addresses wiped off the Internet.?
?I'm pretty sure that when our founding fathers drafted the constitution, the intention was to allow people to express their opinions about religion, politics, and social issues.
?Their hearts were in the right place, but I doubt they could have ever imagined that the 20th century would usher in telephones, radio, television and the Internet.
?Maybe the idea that these new communications tools could allow a person to deliver messages of hate and destruction worldwide would have given the forefathers pause, possibly to add a few caveats to the rights of free speech.?
?Of course the Internet should and could be policed. The responsibility for this rests with us all.?
