It's just a question of sport 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 21-11-1997

 SURVEYS have a way of stating the bleeding obvious. Pick up any popular magazine, particularly those that target GYMs (gorgeous young mums) and you will find plenty of evidence.
It goes along these lines: ?Latest surveys in the US show that people who eat more are fatter than those who do not.?
This sort of shock-horror can pave the way for weeks of survey-related diet stories in which readers are told: ?Eat less and you will lose weight.?
Similarly, a survey could find that people who don't wear sunscreen at noon on a summer's day in Perth are more likely to suffer sunburn than those who do.
Or, people who drink 12 glasses of beer and smoke 30 cigarettes in two hours at the pub on a Friday night are more inclined to be sick in the morning than a Tibetan monk on a retreat.
This week I read a survey in the Melbourne Age about blokes. Researchers at A.C. Nielsen McNair talked to more than 1000 Melbourne men.
One area covered was conversation. The Age reported: ?Women are supposed to be good at this, men hopeless. Our survey results seem to support this: ask men about the most common topic of conversation with their male friends and sport is the knock-out winner, followed by work.
?. . . personal problems, well men don't seem to have any.?
Then followed some interviews about this finding with psycho-babble merchants like professors and psychologists who opined that blokes chose to talk about sport because ?(they) are doing something very subtle . . . It is their "social lubricant' a kind of "sports chatter' that can open a path for deeper intimacies?.
Another said sport was tied to notions of masculinity. It conjured images of being tough and courageous, disciplined and committed. ?It has a blokey, boisterous energy, passion and emotion that we often don't give to our emotional life.?
This survey, then, is a work of sheer genius. I wonder, if blokes are happiest talking about sport, how the subject of answering a survey was broached by the research person.
Presumably he or she bailed up some poor unsuspecting bloke in a pub with the line: ?What about Peter Matera? Reckon he'll go to the Dees? And by the way what do you talk about most? Is it a) the wife and kids; b) your vegie garden; c) the translation of the French literary notion of serie-noir into the more populist film-noir; d) the line of succession to the British throne, or e) footy??
Much of this button-holing is not even that necessary. The gentle art of eavesdropping, er I mean observation, would have revealed similar findings without the need for exhaustive interrogation.
For example research persons could have spent a day in a coffee shop ?accidentally? over-hearing conversations between GYMs.
I am sure they would have found the following fairly typical:
GYM 1: Daarling, ooh I do like your new hairdo. It does so much for your eyes, and goes well with the new shoes. Where did you buy them?
GYM 2: At Leonard's in Subi; you know where that guy massages your toes when he fits shoes. I do wish my Jim would do that. Our life has become so predictable, and little Skye is growing up so fast.
GYM 1: Do you mean the Leonard who's having it off with Trina? Wow, I'd like him to massage my toes. That Trina, all the luck, but it's how society changes . . .
When the GYMs come up for air, the researcher could note: women talk about anything and everything.
The next day could be spent at a pub observing two blokes. It could well go like this:
BLOKE 1: Maaate.
BLOKE 2: Maate. New haircut?
BLOKE 1: Yeah. Beer?
BLOKE 2: Maaaate. Justin Langer, hey?
And thus, very quickly, the researcher would note: Blokes like to talk about sport.
I wonder what other surveys are up the sleeves of the trend-watchers? How about ?Do cats drive dogs mad?? or ?Do children like sweets?? or even ?Do people enjoy paying bank fees??
Time to go. Next week: Can the Spice Girls sing?
