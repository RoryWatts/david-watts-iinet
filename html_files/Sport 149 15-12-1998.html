Ashes dull without good fight 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 15-12-1998

 CHANNEL 9 should tape Tony Greig's sign-off from yesterday's Test coverage. ?So that's the end of the day's play. England in serious trouble,? is all he said.
And that is all he needs to say the way the old enemy disintegrates so regularly.
Nine could save itself space in the commentary box and wages by just replaying the tape - or the England in serious trouble part of it - at random intervals during play.
The network might also consider taping Richie Benaud saying ?that's plumb? and give the doyen's voice a bit of a rest.
The England batsmen's ineptitude against spin will not let him relax otherwise.
The Ashes have become predictable, even dull, viewing. Australians like to see their teams win, but mostly in a good, close fight.
The England collapse on Sunday morning became a yawn. Then the sight of Michael Slater and Justin Langer batting properly on Sunday afternoon simply pointed out the chasm between the two countries.
Today, we can assume the English tail - which appears to start with captain Alec Stewart - will again trudge off with shoulders sloping and pitiful looks as Colin Miller and Stuart MacGill finish the job.
If they don't get them, Glenn McGrath and Damien Fleming surely will.
Earlier in the series, critics were being charitable enough, saying that if England could only hold its catches, it would give Australia a huge fright.
True enough about the catching, but now the batting is even more cause for concern.
And there is more to wreck a couch potato's dream of some good sport. The entire team's state of mind is a worry, according to English experts.
Wisden editor Matthew Engel wrote yesterday that Australia has a more skilful generation of cricketers, as well as a more confident one.
?England, bowed down by years of being told they are rubbish, now play as if they believe it to be true,? he said.
ABC radio guest commentator Jonathan Agnew wrote in yesterday's Guardian about the despair of supporting England in Adelaide.
?We've seen so many of these mind-numbing, depressing days before that just one more should not make any difference. But, once again, a sense of total futility has descended on us all,? he said.
Neither provided any solutions to a potential problem for Nine: that such lop-sided cricket might be a turn-off.
