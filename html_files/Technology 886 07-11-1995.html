McSurf the McNet as Britannica rules Web 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 07-11-1995

 HAGGIS, bagpipes, kilts, Billy Connolly, Glaswegian accents ... depending on your point of view, we have a lot to thank the Scots for.
What we can be thankful for is that in 1768 three canny gentlemen from Edinburgh - Andrew Bell, Colin Macfarquhar and William Smellie - published the first Encyclopaedia Britannica.
As the oldest and biggest English-language general encyclopaedia, it is a byword for authority.
But at more than $1800 for a CD-ROM version, the Britannica is simply unable to compete electronically with the likes of Grolier, Colliers and the omnipresent Microsoft Encarta.
It is on the World Wide Web, however, and with an offer of a seven-day free trial, the Britannica is hard to resist if you are curious or have schoolchildren in the house, needing to know facts accurately and quickly.
Britannica Online, as it is called to distinguish itself from the groaning leather-bound common or garden variety Encyclopaedia Britannica, is up to version 1.3 on the Web.
One of the endearing features is a collection of 17 so-called classic articles by famous authors.
Here, Sigmund Freud analyses psychoanalysis, G.K. Chesterton waxes lyrical about Charles Dickens and Harry Houdini grapples with conjuring.
Another novel feature is for the surfer who is lost for a word or thought. Britannica Online offers a random article. Just click the hyper text and who knows what will appear.
The night I tried I was presented with the life and times of Elisha Graves Otis (1811-1861), a pioneer who invented the safety elevator. Without him we would never feel comfortable in anything over a two-storey building.
Britannica, in US hands since 1901, is as avid a sales pitcher as ever. Browsers have a choice of a demonstration database search, free seven-day trial, or joining up at $US125 a year.
