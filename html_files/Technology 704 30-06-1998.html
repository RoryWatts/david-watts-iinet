Technology takeover turns day into night 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 30-06-1998

 IT'S ALL too much. The pace of the new technological world has finally caught up.
Last week I subscribed to Foxtel, thus adding pay-TV on one set to normal TV on two others, Nintendo64 on one of them, the Internet on the PC and goodness knows how many CD players, digital alarm clock-radios, microwaves and VCRs ... all with different time settings and personalities.
Foxtel tipped the scale from organised chaos to the anarchy of information overload.
Take last Thursday for example ... please.
I connected to the Net, read 27 e-mails (three abusive, two trying to sell me something), read the Age, the Guardian, the Times, checked Reuters, the ABC, BBC and CNN; then, because of Foxtel, searched the channels until I found when Bonanza was on and asked the network to send me an e-mail reminder.
A quick check of my Microsoft Outlook calendar revealed I had missed an important meeting. But not to worry I faxed, then e-mailed the possibly disappointed other party.
I then cruised the Foxtel channels on TV, all 28 of them, and spent an average of one minute on each. This figure was distorted because I watched Dr Who for 10 minutes, trying to work out whether Sylvester McCoy was a better time lord than Tom Baker, William Hartnell, Patrick Troughton or even John Pertwee.
Case unresolved, I cruised to watch some live Wimbledon, then realised that Fox Sports was showing only one match and I had no idea how anyone but Carlos Moya was doing.
Back to the Net and the Wimbledon site for constantly updated scores. A few more e-mails, read the Washington Post and an article by Bill Gates in the Economist, basically saying, I think, no one has the right to interfere with Microsoft's plans for world domination.
The exact recollection was erased when I tapped into the Windows 98 newsgroup and found that someone had sent a list of 25 Web links at which one could find news of Win 98's release that day.
To shrug off this growing technology I rugged up with a familiar, old book, Flying Visits by Clive James. This is a collection of his postcards from around the world written in the 70s and 80s for the Observer newspaper.
Jolly good too, particularly where he describes flying to LA and watching an in-flight movie of Maggie Smith flying to LA for the Oscars in which she is watching an in-flight movie about an actress flying to LA for the Oscars.
Not surprised that that gave James a dose of technology, I put down the book and hopped on the Net to see what impact the urbane and literate Australian ex-pat had made on this new medium.
Searches at four different engines revealed hardly any at all. Just a TV guide talking about an interview he had with Mel Gibson and an amazingly illiterate review by a fan of his book Falling Towards England.
Really, these are some of the reviewer's descriptions of James: ?(he uses) rather elaborate, multisyllabled prose?, ?Australian expatriot?, ?Clive James, the larikan latestarter?.
What the Net has given in the ability to communicate, it would appear to have taken in the method of communicating ... good, grammatical English.
Confounded by the Net, TV and books, I went outside for air. But it was midnight. The day had gone and I had achieved nothing.
Oh, I did get time to write this rubbish ... now for Bonanza.
