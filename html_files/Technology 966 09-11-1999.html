Cautionary lessons of shopping on the Net 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 09-11-1999

 IT IS possible to go overboard about the Internet. It has so much to offer.
Last Saturday afternoon, easily 30 minutes before the television news made its pronouncements, Yes-voting Web users were able to wail ?No? at the updated results at the Australian Electoral Commission Referendum site.
They were even able to note with surprise how the silvertail voters of Kooyong supported the republic model in almost exactly the same numbers as the blue-collar people of Melbourne Ports.
Earlier in the week they could have got the latest on the Melbourne Cup with The West Australian's own TABform Online.
They could have had a cyber-wager with Alice Springs bookmaker Centrebet, which took close to $1 million on the race.
If they went to the reborn Britannica.com for free use of the Rolls-Royce of encyclopedias they might have been tempted to pursue the invitation to pre-order the new CD 2000 version for about $100.
Then, as they went to enter their credit card details at the check-out page, they would have wished they had kept their eyes on the fine print when they noticed this bargain was for US and Canadian buyers only.
And therein lies a tale. Yes, it is possible to go overboard about the joys of the Net, but it pays to remember it is still in a ?wild west? state of development.
This is particularly so with e-commerce. The Net has been a shopping tool for virtually five minutes, after all.
Evidence of just how raw the medium is might be found in the fact that the biggest revenue generator is still pornography. According to a Melbourne Age report on Saturday, more than 10,000 new sex sites are going up every week.
Away from the porn scene, it is often assumed that shopping online with big, established firms is the safe way to go.
A 54-year-old Perth man e-mailed me a couple of weeks ago to tell me that is not always the case.
He told a tale of woe that started last May, with an online order from a prominent US firm for a soundcard.
IT ENDED only last week when the furious online shopper was forced to e-mail a picture of a gold credit card to reclaim money lost by receiving faulty goods from overseas.
In between was a tragedy of unanswered e-mails and faxes, unanswered international phone calls and bills for customs, tax and courier charges.
?You'd be surprised just how many people have been screwed and just given up,? he said. ?We are talking here about ... getting the business from big business.?
His story arrived only days before a www.consult report that pointed out serious problems in e-commerce with 75 per cent of online shoppers abandoning their virtual shopping carts before getting to the check-out page because they were not sure how much they were spending. And then the Financial Times filed a report saying that Internet shoppers suffer from misleading marketing on many leading Web sites.
It is still a wonderful medium for all that.
But the words ?buyer beware? have never been more apt.
