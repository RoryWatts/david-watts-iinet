Pushing art to the limits 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 02-09-1996

 IT MAKES Melrose Place look like Bananas in Pyjamas. The characters live in a comic strip world seething with intrigue, threatened with other-worldly forebodings and they are nearly all up to their necks in fleshy affairs.
?It? is the work of a collective of Curtin University students who are pushing new technology as hard as they can to see what sort of art emerges.
At the moment their energies are concentrating on a steamy multimedia series called hOUSE@ (correct) which is playing on a computer screen near you.
For the past 10 weeks hOUSE@ has been bubbling along, its plot thickening and the images becoming more outrageous and finely tuned.
Publicist for the Curtin group, Sian Binder, says hOUSE@ is really the first Australian Internet soap opera.
She disputes the claim by US software giant Microsoft that it had the first Net soap when it launched a turgid TV-like romp called Friday's Beach at its Australian site last week.
The media hype - and The West Australian admittedly was caught by it - inflamed the Curtin students, who at that time had spent more than eight weeks putting to air their own soap, especially designed for new media.
Ms Binder claims more than 15,000 Net users have seen the soap already.
She said this week that TechTalk, the Sydney-based producers of Friday's Beach, had been e-mailed immediately the claims started about their ?first soap on the Internet.?
She said TechTalk took six days to reply, saying they would look at hOUSE@ before replying further.
The hOUSE@ team's main gripe, apart from what they say is Friday's Beach false claim, is that the Sydney-set soap does nothing more than echo TV soaps, adding only a few of the possible enhancements available through multimedia.
And the Curtin crew is challenging the very idea that television will be the main entertainment vehicle of the future.
hOUSE@ is the students' leadup showcase for a bigger event ... the hOUSE@ gallery and exhibition in October, part of the Artrage 96 festival.
Ms Binder said hOUSE@ would celebrate cyber-art and cyber-artists throughout Australia.
Internationally renowned new media artist Stelarc would make his first Australian appearance at the celebrations for hOUSE@.
The Perth Cyber Theatre dance group would perform, projecting its work via satellite-computer hook-ups around the Net.
And there would even be more conventional fare - such as live music.
The hOUSE@ Internet soap is not just making waves on the Net.
Community radio 6NR broadcasts the latest episode each Tuesday.
The format of the soap - on the Net at least - is like no other serial drama doing the rounds at the moment.
For a start it is not filmed and there are no scripts on paper.
The main characters are all friends of the creators and backroom-workers at hOUSE@.
Ms Binder said the ?stars? were filmed on video before the soap got off the ground.
They went through simple motions such as walking, then went on their merry ways.
Their images, however, were transferred to computer, where for the past 10 weeks they have undergone massive operations such as projecting characters into a virtual reality world beyond the animated street scenes they inhabit normally.
It has also involved some computerised cutting and pasting of other bodies on to the ?stars? so they can perform the mostly hilarious bedroom scenes of the plot.
The result is an addictive little pot-boiler which never fails to amuse and has the soap's main ingredient ... suspense: you've just got to see the next one.
The five scriptwriters - Ms Binder, Kathy Barber, Ben Steele, Nick King and Sam Collins - thrive on late nights, coffee and a sense of the absurd.
Not to mention a healthy addiction for the real Melrose Place for a couple of the writers.
The five writers, plus Alex Yapp, create the artwork and make the new technology work in each episode.
The combined energies of these people from film and television, fine art, electronic computer engineering and computer science disciplines is scheduled to run for another six episodes.
Then their work will go on CD-ROM as another contribution to Artrage.
Other budding cyber-artists are also being given a chance to have their new media work included on the CD.
They can submit details at the hOUSE@ World Wide Web site on the Internet.
