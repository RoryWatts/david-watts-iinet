Winter Games? The less sled the better 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 16-02-1998

 I MUST apologise. In the past few weeks I have made several references to the ?lunatics on the luge? in various articles.
After a week of watching the Winter Olympics from Nagano on Channel 7, I have changed my initially uninformed opinion.
They are not merely lunatics. They are dumb, stupid, definitely certifiable, dead from the neck up ... jokes.
But what fun. For the uninitiated the luge is a small sled. A luger is a person of the above requirements who lies flat on the sled and slides at speeds up to 130kmh down an iceslide (imagine a waterslide but much, much colder).
You can even have a double luge in which one nutter lies on top of the other, sort of like a lopsided sandwich.
Lugers negotiate the descent without perceptible moves. On my reading of the Encyclopedia Britannica's guide to the cool games, they use minute twitches of the shoulders and legs - and no doubt the eyes - to steer them on their way to what looks like certain death.
The winner is not only the fastest over four slides down the 1000m-to-1300m course - time differences are measured in thousandths of a second - but more importantly, the one who has lived to tell the tale.
In Nagano, the solo gold went to Germany's Georg Hackl for the third Games running. Last Monday a small crowd gathered in front of my TV to watch the 31-year-old army sergeant make his first run.
There was stunned silence for the roughly 49.999 seconds that Hackl dived around the Spiral course.
Then this: ?Awesome? (from a 13-year-old girl), ?Unbelievable? (a 12-year-old), ?Oh wicked? (nine-year-old boy), ?Coooool? (another boy), ?Dumb? (40-plus man) and .... (unprintable from another 40-plus man).
The luge provokes mixed emotions then. One is undoubtedly: why bother?
Channel 7 has thrown the kitchen sink and lots of cuddly red and white sweaters at covering these games for little result, judging by the ratings.
That is hardly surprising given the sheer foreignness of the events, no matter how exhilarating or graceful they are.
Ice dancing can be glorious entertainment, but do we want to watch Sandy Roberts interviewing our brave and bubbly 13th-placed Australian duo for more than 10 minutes at peak time?
Or do we need to listen to Neil Brooks trying to grapple with the intricacies of moguls when all the time he sounds exactly like what he is: a swimmer out of water?
And Bruce McAvaney just loves to enmesh himself in sport - here he has been shouting at the speed skating - but aren't we seeing just a teensy bit much of him, particularly with six months of footy just rarin' to go?
Seven has an important commitment to covering Olympic competition, but has taken it a bit far with Nagano.
And no one has yet answered the most important question from the first week: why did both the silver and bronze medallists in the men's snowboarding giant slalom test negative for marijuana?
Surely they are all on something.
