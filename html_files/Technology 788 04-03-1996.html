Flash newcomer fails to match old faithful 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 04-03-1996

 THE newest, flashiest technology failed to make an impact on election night.
The Internet, with its reputation for rapid global communications, did not keep pace with the tally.
Trumpeted as a glimpse into how future election nights might be covered on home computers, both attempts at live Net coverage were about as revolutionary as waiting for Sunday papers.
Live Net coverage was offered at the ABC's election site to complement its comprehensive television coverage.
It was also at a site called Netcetera, which has had a distinguished electronic campaign as a site for virtual voting.
Early indications were that live Net coverage was as good as TV, particularly for expatriates overseas.
As Maxine McKew read the first results from rural NSW about 3.35pm, they appeared on the Net.
But within 45 minutes, Net users took up to four attempts before they could read results - if there were any.
When the ABC team started to rattle off early Victorian figures, they had not been posted on the Net.
Things were no better when Queensland results became available on TV.
But at 5.15pm, it was possible to click on a selection called the Big Picture and get a graph showing how many seats were going to whom.
When WA seats appeared on TV about 6.30pm, not one was on the Net.
But users accessing results shortly after this were greeted by an announcement: 9.30 Keating concedes defeat.
At Netcetera, no results were posted until after 6pm.
As an experiment, live Internet coverage was a failure, but given the rapid rate of change in Net technology, it will possibly be a big player in 1999.
