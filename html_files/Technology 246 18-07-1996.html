Net users' free speech curbed 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 18-07-1996

 SINGAPORE'S brief flirtation with unfettered freedom of speech ended this week when the Government imposed some of the toughest Internet censorship in the world.
The island-state's 100,000 Net users cannot use cyberspace to:
? Bring the Government into hatred or contempt, or excite disaffection against it;
? Mislead and alarm the public;
? Jeopardise security or defence;
? Undermine confidence in the administration of justice.
The Government watchdog, the Singapore Broadcasting Authority, is responsible for determining what constitutes breaches of these rules.
Users or service providers found guilty will lose their licences to operate on the Net.
The laws are seen as repressive in that they will be able to stop criticism of the Government.
An Australian correspondent to an electronic newsgroup - or bulletin board - said the legislation was akin to the Australian Government not allowing Net users to say: ?John Howard is a dork?.
Comments on politics would be reduced to: ?Geez, isn't that nice Mr Howard a peachy-keen Prime Minister.?
The Straits Times newspaper said the censorship was aimed at safeguarding public morals, political stability and religious harmony.
The laws automatically license two groups of Net users - those who provide or sell Internet access and those who provide information with business, political and religious content.
Licensees have to provide to the Government lists of readers targeted by their Net service, and the names of editors, publishers and organisations behind them.
The Straits Times said this was a move to promote responsible use of the medium.
Critics see it as a way of presenting only good news about Singapore to the 40 million Net users worldwide.
The SBA is also responsible for stopping Net users providing objectionable material which it classifies as discussion, or pictures relating to sex, politics or religion.
SBA chief executive officer Goh Liang Kwang told the Straits Times that the laws aimed to encourage responsible use of the Internet, while allowing its healthy development.
Reaction to the censorship has been guarded. The predominantly Singapore-based users of the newsgroup alt.soc.singapore have mostly questioned the effect of the law on them. A few have openly attacked the laws, but other users have warned them to be careful.
