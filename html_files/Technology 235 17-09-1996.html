Footy finals sites make their mark 

Author: DAVID WATTS; NATHAN COCHRANE

Date: 17-09-1996

 THERE is plenty of football finals action on the World Wide Web.
Melbourne radio station 3AW - home of Rex Hunt and Sam Newman - has real audio broadcasts of finals action at the Club 10 World site, run through Foxtel.
The Ultimate Australian Rules site runs results, club logos and features dozens of downloadable pictures of players and some of the greatest marks of the past 10 years.
For pure diversity of choice, 70 Footy Sites allows fans access to club pages, official and unofficial, and basically anything that comes under the Australian football umbrella.

THE Internet has evolved its own rules and sanctions which reflect its technological and social realities in a way alternatives imposed from outside cannot.
So says Electronic Frontiers Australia spokesman Daniel Yee at a site devoted to the thorny issue of Net censorship.
In the text of a speech he gave recently, Mr Yee - a prominent fighter against NSW's failed Net censorship bid earlier this year - puts a strong case for users and not governments making their own rules in cyberspace.
?Governments didn't build the Internet, they don't own it, and they can't control it; they will have to learn to live with this,? he says.

VETERAN US magazine editor and TV producer-turned Web consultant Jerry Lazar has set out what he claims are the top blunders made by commercial Web sites.
Mr Lazar has sought opinions from Web consultants around the world in compiling his list.
Among the blunders are:
?Rushing in without a master plan. Companies are so dazzled by the prospect of establishing a Web presence that they lose sight of the original reasons for being there.
?Appointing the wrong person to the task. In too many corporate boardrooms, this week's VP of New Media is last week's marketing director who has never ventured past e-mail.
?Being too commercial. Internet protocol dictates that you supply free information and entertainment first, and then ask for the sale.
?Using unnecessarily big graphics. The single most frequently heard complaint is that hefty graphics take way too long to download.
