Advertising sure is more than a game 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 14-07-1998

 THE weekend gave armchair sports lovers the perfect opportunity to play the silliest game in town ... that of comparing football codes.
What we saw probably confirmed what we knew already: soccer is the global game, rugby is war between otherwise friendly nations, and state of origin football is a battered concept beloved only of South Australians with chips on their shoulders.
What we on the couch should be thankful for, no matter what code or club we support, was the feast that started with The Allies v Victoria on Friday night, and ended with France shocking Brazil in the World Cup final about 5am yesterday.
TV drama does not get better than watching Australia hold out New Zealand for a rare rugby union win.
But that Bledisloe Cup match in front of 75,000 at the MCG was a shocker to watch. It was not the game's fault. Indeed Australia's 24-16 victory was among the more stirring rugby encounters of recent times, especially Matthew Burke's brilliance.
The coverage by Channel 7 reeked of insensitivity to viewers, however. There were ads during play, not just breaks in play. One Melbourne report said Seven stacked on 16 advertisements in the 15-minute half-time break, barely allowing Gordon Bray, Simon Poidevin or Chris Handy to get in an analytical word.
Considering the match was critically poised at 15-13 to Australia at the time, it was simply an outrageous act.
Seven had trouble of its own even before the game. The MCG's managers, the Melbourne Cricket Club, reportedly threatened to ban the network over a dispute about virtual advertising - the overlaying of sponsors' signs at the ground with other TV-generated ads.
It was also reported in Melbourne that the coverage was in jeopardy because the MCC demanded a share of the television money that the Australian Rugby Union and the New Zealand Rugby Union would be getting.
That is an extension of a drama that at one stage threatened to stop Seven telecasting AFL matches from the MCG, a row that has been resolved for now.
However, the MCC's pleas for the right to determine who can bring cameras to the ground they administer, and to take a slice of the revenue, even has the potential to bring Channel 9 into AFL coverage proper.
The immediate potential is for lawyers to take centre stage and feed on the great contests that the greatest arena in the country hosts.
Unfortunately, sport on TV is more and more about marketing rights and advertising than the great deeds on the field.
The state of origin matches showed you can still have thrilling matches despite losing champions like Peter Matera, Wayne Carey, James Hird, Paul Kelly, Chris Grant and Ben Cousins.
And the under-18 carnival match, won so well by WA against Victorian Metro on Saturday, showed there is a wealth of talent just waiting for its chance.
In one breath, the game is in good hands on the field. But in the next, never doubt that it is men in suits who are pulling the strings.
