Are you a dag or a larrikin? 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 02-12-1996

 A COLLEAGUE told me the other day he believed all journalists were either dags or larrikins. Probably so; and maybe you can categorise the rest of the community the same way.
But first we have to know what a dag is and what constitutes a larrikin.
Dictionaries of Australian slang, or Strine, have no difficulty with the word larrikin. To wit:
larrikin n, v: Larrikin is Australian slang for someone who's fooling around, messing about, or engaging in mischief. The term derives from the verb ?to lark,? which since the Middle Ages has meant - not surprisingly - to fool around, mess about, or engage in mischief.
In simpler times, lads and lasses would spend early mornings trying to catch larks, considered tasty to pre-KFC palates. Many, however, spent their time doing anything but bona-fide ?larking?, and, as a result, the verb took on a cast of impish misconduct. If one says ?larking? with a thick Australian accent, it comes out something like ?larrikin?.
So that is crystal clear. Dag, however, having a lot to do with the unsavoury end of sheep, in particular, is not so easily or readily defined.
A visitors' guide to the Gold Coast, intended for foreign tourists, defines a dag as someone out of fashion. Daggy, it says, means untidy.
Dag is not a pejorative term. It is often applied with affection, unlike those very descriptive Aussie terms drongo, duffer, dingo, dobber, dud or derro.
I have constructed a small questionnaire designed to sort out the dags and larrikins among us. Please try to answer honestly.
1. You are at an outdoor mass at a Catholic school and in your picnic basket are cold cans of beer and tasty nibblies. It is six hours since you ate lunch. What do you do?
a. This is an outrageous question that I will not lower myself to answering.
b. Put my head down and try not to think about it.
c. Waddya reckon!
2. For women: A man you fancy bends over right in front of you to tie his laces. You:
a. Ignore it and look the other way.
b. Have a good long look.
c. Goose him and when he looks up with startled eyes, tell him: ?That's a far better bum than Michael Douglas's in Basic Instinct.?
3. For men: A woman you fancy comes to say goodbye after a party. You:
a. Shake hands.
b. Allow a chaste kiss on the cheek.
c. Grab her, hold her hard against you, kiss her passionately and say: ?There's more where that came from darling.?
4. At the movies for refreshment you:
a. Buy nothing and remain quiet.
b. Choose something soft and quiet like a chocolate bomb.
c. Buy Jaffas and eat them with noisy glee, occasionally rolling a few in dull moments.
If you answered (c) to all these you are probably a larrikin ... or a liar.
If you answered (b) to all you are a dag and probably honest.
If you chose (a) you are possibly lying.
If you had a mixed response then you are not used to these heavily weighted questionnaires and should try again.

OUT OF THE MOUTHS OF BABES: A GYM (Gorgeous Young Mum) of my acquaintance told me of acute embarrassment she suffered at a morning tea for other GYMs of her son's Year 1 class.
She was handing out pikelets or sandwiches or fairy cakes and noticed a lot of smirking and giggling. So she went to the class teacher and asked if she knew what was going on.
The teacher stifled her own guffaws to say that a few days before, the GYM's son was giving the news, and told the class: ?My mum doesn't wear any clothes to bed.? He got no further.
But one of his mates told his mum, who told her friend and so on.
