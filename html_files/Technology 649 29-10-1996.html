Melbourne Cup should be closer than US poll 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 29-10-1996

 TWO nations stop next Tuesday - Australia for a horse race and the US to elect the leader of the free world.
The Melbourne Cup has the power to quieten the heart of cities but has yet to make much of an impact on the World Wide Web.
WestWeb has tried in vain for a week to find a list of all Cup winners since Archer's inaugural victory in 1861. But it does not appear to be among the myriad of punting, breeding, race club and thoroughbred links unearthed.
But there are betting systems out there designed to give punters, professional and mug alike, a Cup tip.
The most fascinating is by Max Hitchins, a marketing man, who in his own words is not a professional tipster and who rarely goes to the races.
However, his Cup site, Fact, Fiction and Fables of the Melbourne Cup, presents a system to pick the Cup winner that has proved quite effective despite its reliance on what can only be called weird science.
Mr Hitchins relies on the winner of the race 100 years ago to give a hint for success. Thus for 1990 he went back to 1890 when the great Carbine won the Cup.
Carbine was called King of the Cup and in 1990 there was an entrant called Kingston Rule ... he picked it and it won.
There are more, similar stories at his site as well as fascinating excerpts of Cup trivia from his book which bears the same title as the Web page.
On a more serious note for punters are the directories of HorseNET, which allow e-mail discussions of Cup fancies.
All Cup-related sites listed here have dozens of links to other horse racing sites, including TABs and online tipping services.
The US presidential race would appear to be a one-horse race. Few pundits are giving Republican hopeful Bob Dole much of a chance against incumbent Bill Clinton.
But there are hundreds of Web resources where opinions differ and many sides of the presidential campaign are discussed.
For sheer authority, the Encyclopedia Britannica's American Presidential Election page is hard to go past.
Britannica has used its considerable and famous resources to bring biographies of all presidents and vice-presidents, explanations of the electoral process and memorabilia through the years.
It also links to some good, serious resources like the Papers of George Washington site, Abraham Lincoln Online and the Franklin D. Roosevelt Library and Museum.
The Skeleton Closet pushes libel laws to the extreme with no-holds-barred looks at candidates' peccadilloes.
