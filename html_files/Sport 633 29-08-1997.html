It's enough to make you sick 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 29-08-1997

 THIS column was supposed to be about funny things that doctors say. It will have to wait. I am angry this week and here is why:
IT MUST be embarrassing to be a Victorian sometimes. Melbourne is actually a nice place despite the pollution and foul weather. But it doesn't take much to sort out the level-headed from the flat-headed . . . or pointy-headed.
The latest catalyst was the Bulldogs-Eagles match last Saturday which acted as the last hurrah for the godforsaken Whitten Oval.
It is now history that six Bulldogs - we probably should call them mongrels -ganged up on the youngest Eagle, Michael Gardiner, before the bounce.
If this act of mass cowardly intimidation was not premeditated, then one can only presume something in the fetid air of the western suburbs got to all six at once.
The immediate wash-up was that Eagle Jason Ball was suspended for two matches for striking Paul Dimattina in the mayhem that followed the mugging of Gardiner.
Both Ball and Dimattina told the Victorian-based members of the AFL tribunal no punches had landed and the Bulldog staged for a free kick. The tribunal decided to accept the umpire's version and throw Ball out.
A week earlier, Nathan Buckley of Collingwood faced a similar charge and the evidence was similar; acting for a free and no contact. What did the tribunal do? Why it accepted the players' versions and Brownlow Medal favourite and adopted Victorian Buckley got off.
What really makes the blood boil, however, is the defence by some Vics - and by former Eagle Karl Langdon on Channel 7 on Wednesday - of the mugging of Gardiner.
A bit of biff, chesting, eyeballing and verballing are all part of the game - if it is one against one and they are roughly the same size. The fighting of such battles is often crucial in winning the football war.
BUT six on one? Gardiner was probably flattered initially. The Bulldogs thought so highly of his obvious talents they just had to unsettle him.
Bulldogs president David Smorgon was obviously at a different game. In the Melbourne Age this week he was reported as saying it was a very good, hard, tough game and he called West Coast complaints distasteful. He said the Eagles had a history of verbal abuse and gang tackles. Maybe so but the question was one of cowardly street-fighter mugging, not tough football.
On the Internet there is a discussion group dealing with footy only. Bulldogs supporters were notably absent from discussions about the game which were dominated by West Australians upset at gutter tactics.
The exception was this Victorian: ?. . . the Bulldogs won. Would you be sooking so much if the West Coast Eagles had won??
Responding to a statement that the Gardiner mugging was cowardice, our Melburnian had this to say: ?Ooooh, look Mummy they're bumping into me. Stop them Mummy. Maybe they should've waited until he had the ball then charged him.?
With support like this there was no need for any other brilliant observations. A few Victorians joined the fray to say they did not necessarily agree with the Gardiner mugging but it was what the Eagles deserved.
They are maddening, people like this. The issue of a young footballer with great talent becoming the subject of gangland terrorism does not seem to be a blot on a great game to them.
My one-eyed view is they are afraid that interstate teams have become the pacesetters and their suburban-based competition on horrible little grounds like Whitten Oval is nearly over.
Well I've got news for them - the AFL has been a true national competition for years now.
And good riddance to the Whitten Ovals over there. Last week's tactics came two days after the emotional unveiling of a statue of late Footscray champion Ted Whitten.
I wonder how proud he would have been of his successors.
