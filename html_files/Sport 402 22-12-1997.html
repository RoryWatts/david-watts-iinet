Test puts welcome spin on Warne waistline 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 22-12-1997

 THE Boxing Day Test against South Africa cannot come soon enough.
Not only will it bring much-needed real sport back to our TV screens, it will also give us the chance to watch Shane Warne bowl in proper cricket - and to see if he is really fat.
But who cares about that last one?
Warne was at the eye of a storm last week, because he walked from a press conference at the unveiling of a wax likeness of himself.
A TV reporter - who said in Melbourne's Age newspaper that he was about 20kg overweight himself - asked Warne if he preferred the slimmer wax version of himself.
What a brouhaha over a few kilos.
I trawled the Internet yesterday to try to find Warne's vital statistics.
An Unofficial Shane Warne Page, written by a fan, said he was 183cm and 85kg (6ft 1in and 13st 5lb).
He might be, but nowhere else could I check it.
The Australian Cricket Board does not deal in such weighty matters and the reason is obvious.
Height and weight do not matter in Test cricket.
Armchair sportsmen probably get kicks from cricket and golf because they are two sports where ?normal? people might think they could stride the stage without appearing ridiculous.
You can't think that about Australian football (it was interesting to note in my Internet search, that three Eagles - Guy McKenna, Brett Heady and Chad Morrison - were all about the same size as Warne).
You could not contemplate that in athletics, swimming, basketball or boxing (though that old bruiser George Foreman is a lump of lard ... but don't tell him).
The perfect physique for a cricketer belonged to Dennis Lillee.
It also belonged to Sir Donald Bradman, Ian Botham, Bruce Reid, Merv Hughes, Glenn McGrath, Warwick Armstrong ... the list of wildly varying bodies is endless.
Where Michael Bevan might be a pocket Hercules, he can't hold a spinning finger to his chubbier mate, Warne.
If Warne's too fat, are McGrath and Jason Gillespie too thin for the big time?
OK, so Warne is about 5kg heavier than he should be.
Is it affecting him? Does it stop our enjoyment of watching him tie a batsman in knots?
Does it stop his aggression, or his give-it-all-for-Australia attitude?
No. He is, in a word, great.
In three words he is great for cricket.
The Test in the MCG cauldron will be as much about his character, as his ability.
The public focus on his waist has unsettled him.
In the past, cricketers such as Hughes - a great mate of Warne's - and Botham, Mike Gatting and Greg Ritchie have all let trivial name-calling go through to the keeper.
Warne is so good and so important to cricket, he would be wise to do the same.
