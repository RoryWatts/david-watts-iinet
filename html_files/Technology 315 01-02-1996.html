Internet rife with anger and abuse 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 01-02-1996

 INSULTS, abuse and anger are dominating political discussions on the Internet.
One Net correspondent claims he has received a threatening e-mail from a person pretending to be Prime Minister Paul Keating.
The ferocity of electronic debate has increased steadily since the March 2 poll was announced.
The newsgroups aus.politics and aus.general are the hotbed of sledging, with 100 or more discussion items posted each day this week.
These groups attracted 30 or 40 messages a day before the election announcement.
Newsgroups are electronic bulletin boards where ideas, advice and insults can be exchanged. More than 6500 newsgroups are accessible to WA Net users.
On Tuesday a correspondent to aus.politics, who calls himself bati, claimed he had received a threatening e-mail signed by Paul Keating.
The e-mail was labelled a crude bid to stifle political debate. Bati said that in the past he had been abused by people with different views and called names by others.
But newsgroup debate usually ended cleanly after a fair exchange of views.
In the ?Keating? case, however, bati claimed that abuse and fear of different views had reached new depths.
The ?Keating? e-mail, which bati says he deleted from his computer in anger after reading, said that lawyers would take legal action against bati because of defamatory statements made in the newsgroup.
Bati wrote that he felt sure the real Mr Keating would not try to kill debate with lawyers.
?The real Paul Keating has far too much personal integrity to do anything like that,? he said.
?I find it absolutely disgusting that any person, in order to attempt to shut up any opponent, uses a method which puts the Prime Minister in such adverse light. He has not deserved that.?
Bati had attacked Mr Keating's policies in previous postings.
Overall, the aus.politics newsgroup has arguments about three to one against the Government.
But in the past two days, more correspondents were posting messages doubting the ability of either major party to run the country.
Political bias is also a hot topic, particularly referring to the graphic interface of the Net, the World Wide Web.
Self-confessed conservative David Moss, a technical officer at the Australian Defence Force Academy in Canberra, runs a site on the Web devoted to Australian politics.
He also maintains a site for the Liberal Party.
Mr Moss wrote in aus.politics that he wanted a Liberal victory.
?Put Labor last,? he said.
A correspondent countered saying Mr Moss should not use Internet facilities at the academy to push his political view. Another correspondent defended Mr Moss's political site, saying it was non-partisan.
The site called Australian politics provides links to political parties and organisations as diverse as the League of Rights and the Australian Republican Movement.
