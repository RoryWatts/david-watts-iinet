Indians not quite up to speed 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 14-12-1999

 SOMETHING is missing at Adelaide Oval. Or maybe that should read someone is missing.
It would appear to be Pakistani fast man Shoaib Akhtar.
Why else would the Channel 9 team make such obsessive use of the speed gun?
Clearly it is missing Shoaib badly. The speed gun was a relevant tool when the boastful young speedster declared he could get to 160kmh.
It was an entertaining sideshow during the three Tests against Pakistan to see Shoaib toss his glossy locks and strain to beat the 150kmh barrier.
And it was equally absorbing to compare and contrast his speed with that of better bowlers such as Damien Fleming and Glenn McGrath.
But Shoaib's gone home now with a cloud over his action as well as a failure to live up to all the hype.
We have the gentler, less tumultuous Indians on our screens. But for some reason we still have the speed gun out at every opportunity.
It is measuring some mediocre stuff such as Michael Kasprowicz's fastest ball of the day compared with the speed of his average delivery.
Or it is telling us that Anil Kumble - who never tires as Ian Chappell never tires of telling viewers - bowls about five to 10kmh faster than rival leg-spinner Shane Warne.
The commentary team, especially Mark Taylor, is spending an inordinate amount of time discussing these figures.
One suspects they haven't got a great deal else to talk about.
Sure there have been wonderful centuries by Steve Waugh and Ricky Ponting, plus the swashbuckling 86 of Warne coupled with his good bowling.
But the characters and combativeness that marked the exciting series against Pakistan are not there.
What we are seeing from Adelaide appears to be a grim, slightly dispirited bunch of Indians who are being outclassed by this tough Australian team.
Look for a shining light to bring some passion and verve and you nearly always end with Sachin Tendulkar.
On Sunday, he occasionally lived up to the hype, particularly when he hit Kasprowicz for three successive fours.
But the little master, whose batting helmet appears two sizes too big, cannot by himself lift India above the ruck at the moment.
His captaincy appears to be the trouble. Indian supporters on the Internet and Indian newspapers have been especially critical in this Test.
Take this comment from a Net discussion group: ?Sachin is a fine bat but he is no leader of men and he is often found wanting in his handling of the bowlers and field placements.?
It has led to an Indian performance best summed up by the Indian Express: ?It is as though the Indian team has been mesmerised by being in Sir Donald Bradman's hometown.?
In fact, the most fascinating part of the weekend's action on Nine turned out to be the trivia question asking how many left-handed batsmen had captained Australia.
