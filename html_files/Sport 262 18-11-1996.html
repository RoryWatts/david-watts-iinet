Test Cricket 

Author: LEN FINDLAY; DAVID WATTS

Date: 18-11-1996

 THE Australia-West Indies Test starting at the Gabba in Brisbane this Friday not only means a resumption of hostilities between two of the world's better sides, it will keep statisticians happy in the peculiar heaven created by the game.
Any form of cricket, but particularly the Test match, creates hundreds of statistics. They can be as simple as Most Runs, Highest Average or Most Wickets, but can become as obscure as instances of brothers scoring a century in each innings or most times a century has been followed by a duck.
We even looked to see whether anyone kept figures on most number of seagulls on the ground after tea on the fifth day. They don't exist ... yet. But you never know.
Today we have some of the standard Test figures as well as many that are not aired often.
First a brief history, as recorded by the Australian Institute of Sport:
Cricket is said to have been played by Saxon Englishmen long before the Norman conquest of 1066. The cricket pitch length of 66 feet is the exact width of a Saxon farmer's holding under the medieval agricultural system. The Saxon word, ?cryce? means a stick and ?wican? means to offer a way through.
?Wican? was also used to describe the wicket gate that closed the entrance to the sheep-pen.
In 1540 the game was introduced to France, and the ?non pair? or umpire was added.
In Australia, cricket was being played regularly in Sydney in 1803, Melbourne in 1838 and between the two States in 1877. Today the game is played by more than half-a-million men, women and children in Australia.
Australia plays Tests against England, South Africa, West Indies, India, Pakistan, New Zealand, Sri Lanka and Zimbabwe.
