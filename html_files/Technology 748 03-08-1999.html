Digital age chat shrinks language 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 03-08-1999

 IN VICTORIAN and Edwardian times, people sent long and well-crafted letters to each other.
Often tied with ribbon and stacked neatly in chests, these formed a valuable archive of what people said and thought.
The telephone eventually replaced letter writing as the preferred way of communication, but thoughts on paper still have been religiously catalogued and stored to maintain the archives of history.
Now we have the perfect medium to keep an active database of our lives.
Want to publish a paper? Put it on a Web page. Want to find out what people think of your ideas? Provide a feedback link for e-mail. Better still, provide a chat forum where people can indulge in a digital conversation.
Such online chats offer a new way to look at events; an active as well as interactive method of recording history.
Great theory, but unfortunately the human race gets in the way. We users have taken to e-mail and chat forums willingly. But we have reinvented the English language to do it.
Examples of the shorthand of the digital age:
?FWIW: For what it's worth.
?IMCO: In my considered opinion.
?JOOTT: Just one of those things.
?ROTFLOA: Rolling on the floor laughing out aloud.
It reminds me of a comedy skit done by both Benny Hill and the Two Ronnies in the 1970s. In it they had a person showing how easy it was to teach English to foreigners as any sentence could be made up of single letters.
The question, ?have you any eggs?? could be written: F U N E X. The answer, ?yes I have ham and eggs?, went: S I F M N X.
That could be the level of communication we are looking at in the digital age.
Last week's Foreign Correspondent on the ABC appeared about to transcend that. It ran a story about the boom in Internet stocks, and focused on the new breed of nerdy millionaires.
Anchorwoman Jennifer Byrne had two of her wired interviewees lined up for an online forum at the show's Web site afterwards. She plugged it several times, appropriately a story about online riches deserved online follow-up.
And the ABC has left a record of the forum for posterity. Here are some highlights copied from the Web record:
?Can anyone explain this kerfuffle??
?What about ME??
?Most interesting Mr Bond. It's a pity you won't live to see my cyberspace bomb.?
There was also a bit of a spat about changing screen colours.
But in the first page of the archived Web forum - which was the last page of the discussion - there did not appear to be much sense apart from a suggestion to resume the chat on a chat channel, or to try ICQ.
It is unfair to single out Foreign Correspondent because many online chats can look like a rabble. And it just might be an accurate reflection of how we communicate with each other.
