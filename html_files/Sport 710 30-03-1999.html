Footy nothing to smile about 

Author: DAVID WATT

Date: 30-03-1999

 YOU can tell that the football season proper has started ... no one is happy.
Mick Malthouse was disgruntled at the way his Eagles midfielders went flat. Damian Drum was upset that the Dockers did not really show up until after quarter time.
Fremantle and West Coast supporters in Melbourne were fuming that the best derby yet was not shown on Channel 7 there, even though Bruce McAvaney told Sportsworld viewers on Sunday morning that it would be.
He also said Sydney v Port Adelaide would be shown ?right around the country?. Perth must have moved.
St Kilda fans had agonised that Seven in Melbourne would show only the second half of its match against Brisbane on Saturday night, preferring Adelaide v the Bulldogs. By half-time, Saints supporters were probably pleased.
Collingwood types were not impressed that Seven chose to ignore the Pies-Hawthorn clash. There was even talk of a conspiracy because Collingwood president Eddie McGuire is a Channel 9 man.
Carlton president John Elliott moaned that the AFL had done little to promote Thursday's season opener against Essendon.
Some grievances were justified; some were just strange, but the unhappiest notes came before the first ball was bounced.
Nicky Winmar was justifiably upset that Sam Newman on Nine's Footy Show had impersonated him by putting on black face make-up.
Its host, the many-hatted McGuire, did not look too thrilled with Newman's performance either. On Sunday, he apologised for it but scoffed at suggestions Newman would be suspended.
Newman's act spoiled what up to that late moment had been a top show. In football parlance, Nine's team had been hard at the ball.
The rare Wednesday night outing pitted the Footy Show against the Jason Dunstall-hosted Live and Kicking on Seven. It was barely a contest as the Nine team threw in top interviews and plenty of analysis against Seven's dancing girls and impersonators (although Matthew Burton's Lurch was not to be missed).
Live and Kicking ironically had Russell Morris singing the Real Thing while the Footy Show was doing the real thing and concentrating on the football.
Where Seven had James Hird talking about Len Thompson selling his Brownlow, Nine was questioning the man himself.
McGuire's highwire act, balancing the presidency against journalism, will be put to the test this season. On what basis can we judge his interviews with Elliott and Melbourne president Joe Gutnick?
Was McGuire an objective journalist or a fellow president with inside knowledge?
The same goes for Terry Wallace on Seven's Talking Footy. How much will he really be able to contribute as a fulltime coach? Will his Bulldogs escape the scrutiny that other clubs will bear?
Stay tuned.
