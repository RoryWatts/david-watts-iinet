Doubt cast on Net doom 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 29-02-1996

 THE Internet was expected to close today but now appears likely to be open for business as usual, despite prophecies of doom.
Warnings of the February 29 ?leap day? closure have been circulating worldwide for two weeks.
The global E-mail warning of closure came from a Kim Dereksen, who purports to be from the interconnected network maintenance staff (main branch) at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in the United States.
His warning said the Net had to be closed for cleaning each Leap Year, on February 29.
The cleaning, he said, eliminated dead E-mail and inactive sites on the Net.
?Five powerful Internet-crawling robots situated around the world will search the Internet and delete any data that they find,? Mr Dereksen wrote.
He advised Net users - 30 million in the US and probably one million in Australia - to disconnect all terminals and networks, shut all Internet servers and refrain from connecting to the Net.
The warning was greeted with scepticism and down-right disbelief on the Net in Australia.
In the newsgroup - electronic bulletin board - aus.general, correspondents exchanged dozens of views about the Leap Day warning.
A posting by one user alerting others to the cleaning day and saying he hoped they did not think it a hoax was answered promptly by one saying:
?I'll enjoy the 29th on the Net while you are huddled in the corner with your computer in its anti-static, anti-internet-crawling robot shielding.?
The original correspondent hit back questioning users about whether they had been on-line in 1992 or whether they had read special directives on the Leap Day warning posted some years ago.
Immediately, about a dozen users responded that they had worked all day on February 29, 1992, without a worry.
Examination of the on-line directives over the years revealed they had all been written on April 1.
The giveaway was that one spoke of a new software protocol called Sonnet to Sonnet Translation - it was signed by ?William Shakespeare, Globe Communications, London?.
?The Internet is not controlled by any central body.
