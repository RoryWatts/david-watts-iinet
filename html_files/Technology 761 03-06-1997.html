Netcaster is new player in browser war 

Author: DAVID WATTS; VICTORIA SHANNON

Date: 03-06-1997

 NETSCAPE has unveiled what it hopes will be its newest weapon in the browser war with Microsoft.
Netcaster is its version of the push technology that delivers Web sites to both the desktop and hard drive with minimal intervention from users.
Unfortunately WestWeb cannot get it to work, despite having adequate hardware - a 133 Pentium and 24Mb of RAM.
The program looks elegant, not unlike Microsoft Encarta's interface, and promises to go out and fetch content from CNN, ABC in the US, Wired magazine and CBS sport, among others.
But it keeps crashing, throwing an invalid page fault.
Netcaster will be central to the new version 4.0 Communicator Net package when it is officially released in the next few months.
At the moment it is in pre-release or beta mode, like the rest of the Communicator line, which last week came out in its fifth version - without Netcaster.
Netscape says Netcaster allows users to subscribe to ?channels? - specially prepared Web sites - and download them directly to the desktop, which it terms the Webtop.
The channels can be updated at user-defined intervals, meaning that Netcaster will download new pages automatically and dis play them on screen if required.

BEATRICE'S Web Guide is a joint site of Women's Wire and Yahoo, the Internet search-and-catalogue company, which purports to bring you to ?only the good stuff?.
It has an extensive ?list of lists?, and Beatrice is a friendly tour guide. Most of the sites are reviewed or at least put into some kind of context. I guess my only problem is I just don't understand why Fashion & Beauty is a women's issue.

THERE are few gentler ways to idle away the hours than to become engrossed in cricket.
An Ashes-related Web site allows users with Java-enabled browsers to select teams and play matches over four innings.
I had a fine weekend. My Australian selection beat England outright by 177 runs, partly due to a mesmerising four-wicket spell by Shane Warne.
But the telling factor was a century in each innings by Mark Taylor. Sometimes miracles do happen ... even if only in the virtual world.
