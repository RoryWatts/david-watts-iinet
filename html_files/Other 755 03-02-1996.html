Labor's Net site comes under attack 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 03-02-1996

 INTERNET users are writing to the Labor Party's official Net site to attack Prime Minister Paul Keating and the Government.
The Soapbox site allows World Wide Web users to fill in a form with comments about political issues which is then e-mailed to the party.
Comments are published electronically at the site.
Before the election was announced, published comments were almost totally pro-Government.
The day the March 2 poll was set there was even an anodyne message from a Victorian supporter saying: ?I would like to wish the Labor Party good luck in the coming election. I think you are going to win.?
But the next message was far less complimentary. Sent on Sunday, it was a plea for a fairer family law system from Emmanuel Theodosiou, president of the Queensland organisation, Children And Parents After Separation.
He accused both Federal and State Labor Governments of destroying the family.
Noting that he was not a Liberal, Mr Theodosiou wrote: ?The policy structure of the ALP seems to be to destroy the family unit and provide "stop gap' measures to pay for lousy policy initiatives.
?Is there anyone out there, sitting member or candidate, who will support families in Australia??
The next published message, again written on Sunday by an Internet user who did not give his name, was virtually a rehash of Labor's winning 1972 slogan ?It's Time?, but with a twist.
Headlined ?Time for a change - vote Liberal!?, the message said: ?As far as I am concerned this country of ours does not have one single decent politician.
?They are all corrupt and live with the philosophy of the rich getting richer and the poor getting poorer.
?Well it's time for a change. John Howard may be no better, or God forbid, an even worse PM than Paul Keating has been ... but that's the chance the people of Australia must take.?
The message ended: ?Liberal and proud of it.?
The Liberals do not have an official Internet site but supporters have set up Net pages outlining policies, history and philosophy of the party.
Comments can be e-mailed, but are not published.
