Day that's the low mark of the week 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 14-08-1998

 TUESDAY is the worst day of the week. On Mondays, there is Talking Footy; Live and Kicking screens on Wednesdays; Thursdays belong to the Footy Show, and from Friday to Sunday the AFL is wall-to-wall.
But on Tuesdays not a handball or a hammie. It is dire, but not Jack Dyer, unfortunately.
This realisation came at the same time it dawned on me I am addicted to the game. Though this has been bleeding obvious for years to those I know and love, it has not been so to me.
Sure I lounge about watching every match and replay all weekend, and only speak to yell ?you idiot?. But I thought everyone did that . . . well, the blokes at least.
I know this behaviour ranks me somewhat below the dog in the affections of the family between March and September. But I thought that was the BO produced by refusing to leave the TV set.
But all became blindingly clear the other Tuesday night.I was sitting and staring on the veranda, there being absolutely no footy to watch, when I realised I was mentally reciting the results of every Eagles game this year.
And I got all the scores right.
Immediately before this procession of ?North 15.13 d Eagles 15.11?, I had been naming the team to win the premiership, given that Andrew Donnelly, Chris Mainwaring and Brett Heady were all fully fit.
And I had been shuffling the side, placing Peter Matera in the forward pocket and Glen Jakovich at full-forward.
I had even cried out: ?But what if David King cuts loose? Will I have to swap Drew Banfield for Roo??
Then I sat down and proved, by carefully analysing every quarter of the year, that West Coast left the rest for dead . . . if the game was only half as long.
When this addiction hit I know not. It may well have been 20 years ago. As a Queenslander just moved to Melbourne, I thought it would be a bit of a wheeze to watch the Richmond-Carlton match that opened the 1978 season at the MCG.
I came away with bright eyes and bushy tail and immediately hired a TV set so I could watch everything to do with the game. I even thought Lou Richards was terrific.
Then again it might have been when I went to Moorrabbin with a mate. ?You've got to have a team, Dave, or everyone will think you're peculiar
. . . or a Queenslander.?
So St Kilda it was, with names like Robbie ?Mad Dog? Muir, Rex Hunt (the same), Trevor Barker and WA's Gary Sidebottom (who we used to call Siddy-bottom).
Maybe it wasn't then, maybe it was in 1991 after returning from England to find an established West Coast lighting up the Perth scene.
Maybe it was only last year when I became a member and was able to get a regular seat at Subiaco and scream ?oh no, Fraser, what are you doing? with the rest of the desperates.
Whenever it was, it has had some devastating side-effects as any sort of addiction does.
LAST week a GYM (Gorgeous Young Mum) friend had the temerity to have her birthday party on the same day as West Coast's critical clash with Sydney. The timing of the bash meant I got to see only the woeful first quarter in which the Eagles bumbled to be 21 points adrift. Then the partygoers had to make merry.
I was miserable. At the restaurant where we partied, a mate and I bribed the waiter to bring us the score. ?Stuff the chicken, son, just give us the result.?
Naturally we killed him quietly because there were Docker fans present when he delivered the news - Sydney by a goal.
But it sounded like a stirring fightback and I was glad that I had set up the VCR to tape the last three quarters.
Party over, it was a mad rush to get to the TV, rewind the tape and get an hour or so fix.
But the tape was scrambled. We had had it 10 years and had taped everything from LA Law to South Park on it in the interim.
I quietly hurled it into the backyard with a scream of ?you idiot?.
Now I need a big fix. Thank God it's Friday.
Come on Dockers, save Gerard's hide.
