Computer cool to suitor's advances 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 04-03-1997

 MY COMPUTER and Windows95 celebrated their first anniversary last week. Somehow they managed to stay together.
It has been a rocky relationship and there have been periods of separation. The computer's old suitor, Windows3.1, has had to move back in more than once.
But I've learnt to reformat a hard drive and re-install everything I need in a shade under four hours ... so that's a slight compensation.
To start at the beginning: the computer was an IPC ValueMagic with a 486 DX4/100 processor, 8MB of RAM and a 540MB disk. It didn't start the day it was installed in July 1995.
But a week or so later, an hour before IPC announced it was closing retail outlets in WA, we took delivery of a PC that worked.
Within a month it needed a new CD-Rom drive, but apart from that no real worries. It chugged along with Win3.1.
But after gliding through computer store after computer store, and listening to the cyber-hype of Internet users with 32-bit connections, Java and multi-tasking, I just had to have a Start button. So I parted with $159 at Dick Smith's in William Street and cast Win3.1 aside.
The marriage started well. Win95 was happy on the IPC and the IPC did not complain.
But when I hooked up to the Net a cloud no bigger than a man's hand started to hover.
Win95's hardware detection could not find a modem for hours. I finally told it that I had a new one and to install it. With much agonising it did and I connected to the Net and downloaded 32-bit Netscape, Eudora, Free Agent and Cute FTP.
None worked. Many messages, faxes, emails to IPC in Singapore (never answered) and mumblings over beers to mates followed before it was learnt the IPC had a soundcard and inbuilt modem - called a T-32 card - which did not want to have anything to do with 32-bits.
In May the soundcard crashed. To use a technical expression, it packed it in; wouldn't make a peep. With it went the modem and CD-Rom drive which were built in.
About $750 later my IPC emerged with a Creative Soundblaster soundcard and CD-Rom plus a Netcomm Roadster modem.
The next two months were happy. The IPC and Win95 chirped cheerfully.
But in early August the dark cloud that seemed to have disappeared reared again, bigger and uglier than before.
Three or four times a day the IPC would just stop as Win95 flashed a blue screen saying Fatal Exception Error.
I put in another 8MB of RAM, believing that memory was all Win95 wanted.
The 16MB of RAM just made the system crash faster. Another reformat and reinstall. This time happiness prevailed for just one month.
Through November and December I reinstalled Win95 almost weekly.
On New Year's Day with the temperature roasting at over 40C and friends relaxing in pools, I reformatted and installed once again ... reliable Win3.1 was back. A month of stability and I got the crazy urge to reinstall Win95. Win3.1 might be a steady old system, but it makes you feel like you need a chimney coming out of the PC.
This time Win95 held for a week. Then blue screens and total stops forced me to do what I'd suspected for months. I had a new motherboard, Pentium 133, graphics card and extra 8MB of RAM installed.
Now the IPC and Win95 are truly happy. I'm another $660 poorer ... the price of peace and progress.
In retrospect ...
Use computers and computer components with proved track records.
The more RAM the better. Microsoft says on the Win95 pack that it will operate with 4MB. This is very optimistic. Try 16MB to get near a guarantee of stability.
The newer and faster the processor the better. Again, Win95 will work on a 486, but thankfully Pentium 133 or 166 with 16MB RAM is now standard for new systems.
Backup the system registry regularly (the Win95 handbook tells how).
Do regular virus scans.
Perform scandisk and defrag weekly.
Backup important data. If you are a regular Net user, back up your bookmarks and mailboxes.
Find people you trust who are computer-savvy enough to advise you.
Don't be frightened to ask for help.
