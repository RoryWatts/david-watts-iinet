Web crusader puts Hanson back in perspective 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 11-08-1998

 A SYDNEY businessman is using the World Wide Web to try to assure Asians that Pauline Hanson's One Nation party does not represent the views of most Australians.
Trevor Johnson, a 37-year-old management consultant and passionate Internet user, has published a web site called An Open Letter to the World Asian Communities.
Mr Johnson says he wants one million visitors to the site to read his message that Australians are not racist and that One Nation is an unrepresentative minority party.
Visitors to the site are taken point-by-point through Mr Johnson's views on Ms Hanson. He says:
?Asian media often exaggerate Ms Hanson's views and influence;
?One Nation has no similarity with the Ku Klux Klan, other than for racist attitudes;
?Ms Hanson has no power whatsoever. She is but one of more than 200 Federal parliamentarians;
?She is equally detested by both the Liberal-National Party Government, and the Labor Party Opposition.
Mr Johnson decided to go on his anti-Hanson web quest mainly because he believes the major parties are not doing enough to play down the Hanson factor overseas. His business has a predominantly international clientele, and he has many Asian friends.
?Whenever I "meet' anyone in an Asian chat room (on the Net), the moment I introduce myself as being an Australian, the first thing invariably they want to know is about Pauline Hanson,? Mr Johnson said in an e-mail interview.
?She is the only thing many people even know about Australia - certainly more famous than any other political figure in Australia and even more well known than the Sydney Harbour Bridge or the Opera House.
?Her fame abroad is not good news. As much as I detest her blatant and her "latent" racist attitudes, I must say that some sections of the Asian media have exaggerated her out of all proportion, to the definite detriment of Australia.?
Mr Johnson gave examples where friends from Singapore and Indonesia had decided against either holidaying or settling in Australia because they believed racist attitudes fanned by Ms Hanson now prevailed.
?I've also chatted with probably a dozen or more students who were going to study at Australian universities but, due to the recent media attention, have decided to study in their own countries instead, or in a couple of cases, go to London,? Mr Johnson said.
?Business connections in Malaysia have advised me that, in the wake of the Queensland elections, they have been strongly advised by their government against doing business with Australia.?
His Open Letter site links to several anti-Hanson pages and welcomes feedback from visitors.
