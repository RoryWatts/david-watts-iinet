Variety of sites to follow UK election 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 25-03-1997

 IF BRITISH Labour leader Tony Blair was a racehorse he would be a ?dead cert? to win the prime ministership in the general election on May 1.
The latest opinion polls have Mr Blair's party between 25 and 28 points ahead of John Major and his embattled Conservative Party.
Mr Major is battling allegations of corruption within Tory ranks, has had to manage crippling divisions on European policy, and still has to contend with interference from predecessor Margaret Thatcher.
He called the May 1 poll just over a week ago to give the Tories six weeks of campaigning aimed at finding Labour's and Mr Blair's weak spots. It has not gone well so far.
Followers of British politics have a wide variety of Web sites to choose from to follow what should be an historic election if Labour wins as expected.
Some of the best resources are at sites run by heavyweight UK newspapers. The Guardian, Times and Daily Telegraph all have special election sites. The Guardian's is easily the best.
Unlike the other two broadsheets, the paper does not simply echo its printed version on the Web.
Instead, it has developed a sometimes quirky site which not only picks the eyes out of its own excellent and often insightful print coverage, but also extends it to putting the election in a historical context.
It does this by running images of front pages from elections since Clement Attlee ousted Winston Churchill in 1945, and providing the news stories of the time.
Elsewhere both the Press Association - Britain's leading news service - and the influential and authoritative Economist magazine also provide good looks at main policies and players.
Both Labour and the Conservatives have well established sites.
