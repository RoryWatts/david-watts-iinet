Users fired up at threat to freedoms 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 02-02-1999

 TWO developments in the past fortnight have had traditional computer and Net users fuming.
I say traditional because they seem the type of people who regard the Net as a medium which guarantees freedom of access and information, rather than a freedom to cash in, which is currently overshadowing technology and Net-related news.
The first development concerns Microsoft and beta testing of its Office 2000 suite of software.
A correspondent in a Perth newsgroup raised the alarm here when he recently posted a news item written in Windows Update magazine by news editor Kathy Ivens.
Ms Ivens described Office 2000's registration process as ?less than enchanting?, which is already understatement-of-the-year material.
To combat software piracy, Microsoft has introduced enforced registration.
The registration function lets a user launch the software 50 times without registration before it shuts down.
As Ms Ivens says: ?After that, it's over. You can't use your software. You paid for it, you own it, but you can't use it. I hate this. If I pay for a product, I want to be able to use it.?
Her argument was that the decision to register is totally up to the user ... not Microsoft.
She objected to entering her name and address on a form, an action she said almost guaranteed junk mail.
Ms Ivens also rejected arguments that she had bought a licence, not a product, by likening enforced registration to making it compulsory for someone else to start your new car.
The enforced process writes registration information back to your system and checks this when an attempt is made to launch the software.
?The information is based on several elements, including data taken from the system hardware. If you change hardware, you eliminate the validity of your registration,? she wrote.
?Hello? Has no hard disk ever died at Microsoft? Has nobody ever upgraded to a bigger, faster computer??
And, she says, if you can't register over the Net, you can register by phone - but only during normal business hours at Redmond, in Seattle. Office hours there would be between 1am and 9am in WA.
The second upsetting development was the enforced closure of the popular Web site Lyrics.com.
The International Lyrics Server, a site containing the words to more than 100,000 songs, was closed after music publishers accused the Swiss-based operators of copyright violations.
Police seized computers used to serve the site and a prosecutor confirmed that a criminal investigation was under way.
Lyrics.com received about one million hits a day from 100,000 people seeking the words to popular songs, according to Pascal de Vries, a network consultant in Basle who founded the site in February 1997.
Complaints about the freedom to obtain lryics were made on behalf of Warner/Chappell Music Inc, Polygram Music Publishing Inc and six other music-publishing companies. They all claimed they were losing money because people were downloading lyrics instead of buying sheet music.
Lyrics.com gets its lyrics from Net users themselves. It, like the Internet Movie Database, asks people to pool their knowledge.
Surfers at Lyrics.com contributed between 200 and 300 songs a day.
Reaction to this heavy-handed use of authority has been almost universal.
