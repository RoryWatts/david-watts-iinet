Alston's state of e-nation challenged 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 07-12-1999

 THE Federal Government's second Online Australia Day has provoked wildly different views about the state of the Internet in this country.
Communications Minister Richard Alston said the day showed Australia was developing a vibrant online economy and community.
But Roger Clarke, an e-commerce consultant and visiting fellow at the Australian National University, used the day to launch a series of attacks on the Government's online policies.
He claimed at a Web site set up as a ?state of the e-nation? report that the Government had botched its responsibilities.
In a summary of 10 points of criticism, Mr Clarke said:
?Positive things the Government was doing were moving at glacial pace.
?It had failed to act in many vital areas.
?But in the regulatory arena it was making every available mistake ?as quickly as possible?.
Online Australia Day was held on November 26 with its focus point a Web site that Senator Alston said attracted 1.7 million hits. The day was designed to raise community awareness about the employment, business, social, educational and entertainment opportunities on the Net.
Mr Clarke used the first online day last year to issue an upbeat assessment of Australia's Net future.
But this year he said: ?I'm downcast, because the Commonwealth Government is making a complete botch of its responsibilities.?
Among his concerns this year were:
?The Electronic Transactions Bill 1999 covering evidence and signatures would not operate until mid-2001.
?The Privacy Protection (Private Sector) Bill still had not been introduced. It was also unlikely to provide real protection required for public confidence in e-commerce.
?The Government Public Key Authority had failed to build privacy protections into the public key infrastructure.
? Australia's overseas connectivity was inadequate with ?woeful bandwidth and tortuous paths? to Asian trade partners.
?Prices for connections and traffic were exorbitant compared with the US and Canada.
?ISDN was so expensive that it was hardly used.
?Cable passed about 2 million mainly affluent people, yet competitive Net access via cable was unavailable.
?The censorship regime was based on technical ignorance, and would be intrusive, ineffective and costly.
Senator Alston's assessment of the Net, based on the experience of online day, was significantly upbeat, by comparison.
He said that a survey run in conjunction with the day found that Australians stranded on a desert island would prefer to have a computer with Net access than a television or a mobile phone.
?The survey also found that nearly 70 per cent of respondents would now much rather give someone they had just met their e-mail address than their telephone number,? he said.
He hailed online day as a big success, saying that the 1.7 million hits showed that more families were discovering what the Net had to offer.
