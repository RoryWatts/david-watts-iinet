No cheer in empty outer 

Author: DAVID WATTS

Date: 17-02-1997

 EMPTY vessels make the most sound, it is said. But empty cricket grounds must run a close second.
This is so on television news during the sport segments at the end and applies to Sheffield Shield and domestic one-day matches.
Rapturous cheering and loud clapping accompanies cover drives and catches, loud and long oohs and gasps attend bouncers, body blows and near-misses.
The trouble is the cameras capture no one there making these noises. They sweep over rows and rows of empty seats while the sound suggests a crowd gone mad. If it is a ground like the Gabba, where multi-coloured seating is used, the illusion is that thousands are there, particularly in a quick camera sweep.
The reality is that the sound microphones are often focused on a few hundred people in the grandstand, concentrating the sound and giving an illusion of mass excitement.
Noisy, empty grounds are more noticeable on Channel 10's Sports Tonight because that flawed but necessary show screens highlights of all the sport it can get. So a shield segment may have two or three games in a row and the impression is magnified.
It is one of modern Australia's great sporting shames that shield and domestic one-dayers attract so few people. Unreal sound does nothing to change that. Like canned laughter, it just makes things seem sad and hollow.
Yesterday Nine had the one-dayer between Tasmania and South Australia, and no amount of glamorisation would have added even the remotest interest for a WA viewer.
It was a little like those rainy, cold Sunday afternoons on which sports lovers have been forced to digest Fitzroy-Sydney as their live AFL match of the day. (That was when Fitzroy was still alive and Sydney was shocking).
But bless Nine for allowing Kim Hughes, Craig Serjeant and co to bring WA v Queensland into the living rooms for a couple of hours at least. We saw a tense and absorbing battle for first innings points go to the Warriors.
Meanwhile, Tony Greig, Bill Lawry and crew had to flog never-ending ads while letting non-interest pyjama games intrude every now and then. Surely they'd be happier in South Africa watching Mark Taylor try to redeem himself.
Nine's South African cop-out helps Channel 7 steal the limelight, of course. For the second week running Seven has had the most dramatic sport on the box.
Last time it was Pat Rafter's Davis Cup fightback. Yesterday the Australian Masters at Huntingdale had heartbeats racing all day, culminating in a play-off between Peter O'Malley and eventual winner Peter Lonard.
These similar-looking beefy blokes would probably be right at home in a rugby scrum or at the front bar, but they play exciting golf.
It was like a prize fight with platitudes added by Sandy Roberts (?it's historic, it's great, it's happening).
Thankfully Jack Newton and Bruce Critchley were on hand in the commentary box.
?He'll need intestinal fortitude here,? said Newton at one point.
?I don't think you need such big words Jack. It'll take guts,? replied Critchley.
